# Marketing for L&D — Coaching Scaffold

## Why L&D Needs Internal Marketing

Most L&D teams build good programs and then wonder why enrollment is low, completion drops after week one, and executives don't remember what L&D delivered last quarter. The problem is not the product — it's the lack of a launch strategy.

Internal marketing is not promotion for its own sake. It is the **intentional management of learner awareness, motivation, and social proof** so that your program reaches the people who need it and they actually show up ready to engage.

---

## The 4 Learner Persona Types (for Campaign Segmentation)

Don't market the same message to everyone. Segment by motivation and readiness:

| Persona | Profile | What moves them |
|---------|---------|-----------------|
| **Champions** | Intrinsically motivated, trust L&D | Exclusive early access, recognition, peer teaching opportunity |
| **Skeptics** | Competent, don't see the point | Evidence of ROI, peer testimonials, manager endorsement |
| **Mandated Learners** | Required to attend, low intrinsic motivation | Clarity on stakes + WIIFM; make it easy, not preachy |
| **Manager-Activated** | Would engage if their manager surfaced it | Manager enablement kits; make it easy for managers to recommend |

## 3-Phase Launch Framework

### Phase 1 — Pre-Launch (Awareness + Anticipation)
*2–3 weeks before launch*
- Tease the problem being solved, not the course title
- Manager briefing: send talking points and a recommended action ("Ask your team about X challenge")
- Soft registration open for Champions; create scarcity signals ("first cohort limited to 25")

### Phase 2 — Launch (Activation)
*Week of launch*
- Learner email: lead with the outcome they'll gain, not the format
- Manager activation: direct ask to managers ("Your team can register here; here's what to say")
- Slack/Teams moment: peer-visible social proof ("Welcome to the first cohort…")

### Phase 3 — Post-Launch (Sustain + Nurture)
*Ongoing after launch*
- Week 1: acknowledgment and quick win reinforcement
- Week 2–4: manager nudge ("Your team member completed X — here's a conversation starter")
- 60 days post: L3 check-in survey surfaced to learners ("Here's what changed after you completed…")
- Quarterly: aggregate social proof report shared with leadership ("127 team members upskilled in Q1")

## Measuring L&D Marketing Effectiveness

Beyond enrollment and completion, track:

| Metric | What it signals |
|--------|----------------|
| Registration rate (registrations / eligible population) | Awareness + perceived relevance |
| Drop-off by phase (pre-launch vs. enrolled vs. completed) | Where the campaign breaks down |
| Manager activation rate | Whether manager channel is working |
| NPS or CSAT trend across cohorts | Whether reputation is growing |
| Organic referrals ("I heard about this from a colleague") | Word-of-mouth signal; most reliable growth indicator |

---

## The Single Most Common Mistake

**Launching a course without a campaign.** An email with a link is not a launch strategy. Courses launch; campaigns build learning cultures. The difference is whether learners experience a coordinated sequence of touchpoints that build motivation before, during, and after the program — or a single announcement that competes with 40 other emails.

---

## One-Page Campaign Plan Structure

1. **Program name and target audience**
2. **Business problem this solves** (1 sentence)
3. **Learner persona mix** (which of the 4 types dominate this audience)
4. **Pre-launch actions** (what, who, when)
5. **Launch actions** (what, who, when)
6. **Post-launch actions** (what, who, when)
7. **Success metrics** (3–5 KPIs with targets)
8. **Manager enablement plan** (what managers will receive and be asked to do)
