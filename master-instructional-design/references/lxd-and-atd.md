## 1. What is Learner Experience Design (LXD)?

LXD is a human-centered, experience-first approach to designing learning that draws from:
- **Instructional Design** — learning science, objectives, assessment
- **UX Design** — usability, interface, journey mapping, prototyping
- **Service Design** — touchpoints, ecosystems, emotional journey
- **Psychology** — motivation, emotion, identity, self-determination theory

The key shift: LXD asks **"What is the learner experiencing?"** — not just "What are they learning?"

LXD considers the **entire arc**: before, during, and after the formal learning event. It designs every touchpoint — the email invite, the loading screen, the manager follow-up, the performance support tool on the job — as part of a coherent experience.

**Core reference**: *Learning Experience Design* — Niels Floor (2021); *Design Thinking for Training and Development* — Sharon Boller & Laura Fletcher (2020)

## 2. LXD vs. Instructional Design — Key Distinctions

| Dimension | Traditional ID | LXD |
|---|---|---|
| Primary lens | Content delivery | Learner experience |
| Starting point | Learning objectives | Learner needs + emotions |
| Scope | The course | The full journey |
| Methods | ADDIE, SAM | Design thinking, journey mapping, prototyping |
| Success metric | Post-test score | Behavior change + experience quality |
| Stakeholder | SME + sponsor | Learner as co-designer |
| Output | Course, module | Experience ecosystem |

**Coaching note**: LXD doesn't replace good ID — it wraps around it and extends it. The best LXD practitioners have a strong ID foundation and know when to apply which lens.

## 3. The Learner Journey Framework

Map the learner experience across five phases:

### Phase 1: Discover
How does the learner first encounter this learning opportunity?
- Is the communication compelling and relevant to them?
- Does it speak their language, not the organization's?
- Is access frictionless or full of barriers?
- Touchpoints: Email invite, manager conversation, LMS catalog page, push notification

### Phase 2: Prepare
What happens before the learning starts?
- Is there a pre-work strategy? (Flipped classroom, pre-assessment, context-setting)
- Does the learner know what to expect and why it matters to them?
- Are logistical barriers removed? (Tech check, schedule, clear instructions)
- Touchpoints: Pre-work content, calendar invite, preparatory job aid

### Phase 3: Engage
What happens during the learning experience?
- Is the experience active or passive?
- Does it build on what they already know?
- Are there moments of productive struggle, reflection, and connection?
- Is the emotional arc designed — not just the content arc?
- Touchpoints: Course/workshop, scenarios, discussions, practice activities

### Phase 4: Apply
What happens immediately after learning, back on the job?
- Is there a transfer strategy, or does learning evaporate?
- Are managers primed to support application?
- Is performance support available at the moment of need?
- Touchpoints: Job aids, manager coaching guide, application challenge, check-in nudge

### Phase 5: Sustain
What happens over time?
- Is there spaced practice and retrieval built in?
- Is there a community of practice or peer learning structure?
- Is progress visible and recognized?
- Touchpoints: Spaced repetition emails, cohort discussions, recognition, Level 3 evaluation

## 4. Human-Centered Design Methods for LXD

### Empathy Mapping
A collaborative tool for deeply understanding the learner. Four quadrants:
- **Says**: What do they verbalize about this topic? (interview data)
- **Thinks**: What are their beliefs, assumptions, fears? (observed/inferred)
- **Does**: What behaviors do we observe on the job?
- **Feels**: What emotions are present? (anxiety, boredom, resistance, excitement)

Use empathy maps to challenge assumptions stakeholders hold about learners, and to surface emotional barriers that content alone won't address.

### Learner Persona Development
Go beyond demographics. A strong learning persona includes:
- Role context and day-in-the-life
- Prior knowledge and experience with this topic
- Motivation orientation (intrinsic vs. extrinsic; autonomy vs. compliance)
- Emotional relationship to the subject matter
- Tech comfort and access constraints
- What they're afraid of, hoping for

### Learner Journey Map
A visual artifact showing the full experience arc. Columns:
- **Phase** (Discover, Prepare, Engage, Apply, Sustain)
- **Touchpoints** (what they interact with)
- **Actions** (what they do)
- **Thoughts** (what they're thinking)
- **Emotions** (emotional state — use an emoji or feeling scale)
- **Pain Points** (friction, frustration, confusion)
- **Opportunities** (design interventions that could improve the experience)

### Prototyping and Co-Design
- Paper prototype key interactions before building
- Involve learners as co-designers — learner panels, focus groups, beta testing
- "How Might We" (HMW) questions to generate solutions: "How might we help a skeptical learner see the relevance of this compliance training?"
- Fail fast and cheap — test paper storyboards, not finished courses

### Service Blueprint
For complex blended or organizational learning programs: map frontstage (what learners see) and backstage (what happens behind the scenes — facilitator prep, LMS config, manager briefing) and the support processes connecting them.

## 5. Designing for Emotion, Identity & Belonging

### Why This Matters
Learning doesn't happen in an emotional vacuum. Negative emotion (anxiety, shame, boredom, threat) activates the amygdala and impairs consolidation. Positive emotion, curiosity, and psychological safety enhance encoding and transfer.

**Key research**: Mary Helen Immordino-Yang's work on emotion and learning; Claude Steele's research on stereotype threat; Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan).

### Design Principles
- **Psychological safety first**: Design the emotional environment before the content environment
- **Relevance is felt, not stated**: Don't tell learners why it matters — show them in a context they recognize
- **Identity-affirming design**: Use diverse representation; avoid scenarios that implicitly center one demographic
- **Autonomy support**: Offer choice, minimize controlling language ("must," "required"), explain the rationale
- **Competence scaffolding**: Design for early wins; calibrate difficulty to build confidence before challenge

### Stereotype Threat in eLearning
Demographic stereotypes activated before or during learning depress performance. Mitigation:
- Diverse, realistic characters in scenarios
- Avoid gender/race-coded "right answer" characters
- Value-affirmation activities for high-stakes learning
- Frame challenges as normal, not as tests of inherent ability

## 7. ATD Talent Development Capability Model — Master Reference

The ATD TD Capability Model (2020) defines what talent development professionals need to know and do to be effective. It replaced the Competency Model and underpins the CPTD credential.

### Three Domains, 23 Capabilities

**Domain 1: Building Personal Capability** (foundational to the practitioner)
**Domain 2: Developing Professional Capability** (the work of TD)
**Domain 3: Impacting Organizational Capability** (strategic/systems level)

---

## 8. The 23 TD Capabilities — Detailed

### Domain 1: Building Personal Capability

**1. Communication**
Proficiency in written, verbal, and visual communication. For ID: clear design documentation, stakeholder communication, effective facilitation of design reviews.

**2. Emotional Intelligence & Decision Making**
Self-awareness, empathy, managing reactions under pressure. Essential for navigating SME relationships, stakeholder pushback, and ambiguous project requirements.

**3. Collaboration & Leadership**
Working across functions; influencing without authority; leading project teams. ID practitioners frequently lead without formal authority.

**4. Cultural Awareness & Inclusion**
Designing learning that works across cultures; recognizing bias in content, characters, and examples; building equitable learning experiences.

**5. Project Management**
Planning, scoping, managing timelines, risks, and resources. Strong ID practitioners own their projects end to end.

**6. Compliance & Ethical Behavior**
Copyright, accessibility law, data privacy, ethical use of learner data. Especially critical in regulated industries.

**7. Life-Long Learning**
Modeling and practicing continuous development; staying current with learning science, technology, and organizational trends.

---

### Domain 2: Developing Professional Capability

**8. Learning Sciences**
Deep understanding of how people learn: cognitive load, memory consolidation, retrieval practice, motivation, transfer. The scientific foundation for all design decisions.

**9. Instructional Design**
Core ID practice: needs analysis, objective writing, content design, assessment design, evaluation. The technical heart of the profession.

**10. Training Delivery & Facilitation**
Effective delivery of ILT/VILT; facilitation techniques; managing group dynamics; adapting in real time. Not just for facilitators — IDs must understand delivery to design for it.

**11. Technology Application**
Authoring tools, LMS, LRS, video tools, collaboration platforms, emerging tech (AI, VR/AR). ID practitioners must be technically proficient.

**12. Knowledge Management**
Capturing, organizing, and making organizational knowledge accessible. Connects to performance support and LXD strategy.

**13. Career & Leadership Development**
Designing and supporting career pathways, leadership programs, coaching and mentoring frameworks.

**14. Coaching**
1:1 coaching skills for managers and leaders; also the coaching posture ID practitioners bring to SME and stakeholder relationships.

**15. Evaluating Impact**
Designing and executing evaluation at all Kirkpatrick levels; translating data into actionable insights; reporting to stakeholders.

**16. Change Management**
Supporting organizational change through learning strategy; understanding resistance; designing for adoption.

---

### Domain 3: Impacting Organizational Capability

**17. Business Insight**
Understanding how the business makes money, what drives performance, how L&D connects to strategy. Critical for moving from order-taker to strategic partner.

**18. Consulting & Business Partnering**
Diagnosing performance problems, influencing stakeholders, scoping work, contracting. The performance consulting skill set.

**19. Organization Development & Culture**
Systems thinking; culture as a learning environment; OD interventions; team effectiveness.

**20. Talent Strategy & Management**
Workforce planning, succession, talent pipelines. How L&D connects to broader HR strategy.

**21. Performance Improvement**
Human performance technology (HPT); analyzing root causes; designing multi-pronged interventions (not just training).

**22. Diversity, Equity & Inclusion**
Embedding DEI into learning strategy, design, delivery, and evaluation — not as add-on content but as systemic practice.

**23. Future Readiness**
Anticipating shifts in technology, workforce, and business; designing adaptable learning systems; learning foresight.

## 9. CPTD Credential — Exam Prep & Recertification

### Eligibility
- **CPTD**: 5 years of TD experience, broad and strategic scope of work
- **APTD** (Associate): 3 years experience, more tactical/delivery-focused

### Exam Structure (CPTD)
- 150 questions (125 scored + 25 unscored pilot items)
- 3.5 hours
- Covers all three domains and 23 capabilities
- Scenario-based questions — not just recall; apply concepts to situations

### Study Strategy
1. **Download the ATD Capability Model handbook** — this is the blueprint; every question traces back to it
2. **Self-assessment**: Rate yourself on each of the 23 capabilities; identify gaps
3. **Focus on application**: Most questions are scenario-based; practice applying concepts, not memorizing definitions
4. **Domain 2 is heaviest**: Learning Sciences, Instructional Design, Technology Application, and Evaluating Impact are the most heavily tested
5. **ATD Study Hall**: Official prep tool with practice questions mapped to capabilities
6. **Study groups**: ATD chapter study groups significantly improve pass rates

### Key Concepts Frequently Tested

The exam is scenario-based — not recall. Questions present realistic situations and ask what an experienced TD practitioner would do. The most tested capabilities: Evaluating Impact (Kirkpatrick/Phillips), Instructional Design (Bloom's, needs analysis, CLT/multimedia principles), Learning Sciences (memory, transfer, motivation), and Technology Application (SCORM/xAPI, LMS selection). Domain 2 carries the most weight.

### Recertification (every 3 years)
- 60 Professional Development Points (PDPs) required
- PDPs earned through: ATD events, external training, publishing, presenting, volunteering, self-directed learning
- Document in ATD's online portal; keep evidence for audit
- Ethics requirement: Complete ATD's ethics training in each recertification cycle
