## 1. The Academic Landscape of Instructional Design

The field goes by many names across universities: Instructional Systems Technology (IST), Instructional Design and Technology (IDT), Learning Design and Technology (LDT), Educational Technology (EdTech), Instructional Systems Design (ISD), Instructional Technology (IT), and Learning Experience Design (LXD). All refer to overlapping but distinct emphases within the same professional field.

**Two orientations in the field:**
- **Systems/Engineering orientation** (Indiana, FSU, Purdue): Emphasis on systematic design, performance improvement, HPT, and measurable outcomes. Roots in behavioral psychology and military training systems.
- **Constructivist/Learning Sciences orientation** (Vanderbilt, Michigan State, Columbia Teachers College): Emphasis on problem-based learning, situated cognition, knowledge construction, and collaborative inquiry.

Most graduate programs blend both, with the balance varying by faculty composition and program philosophy.

**Three credential levels:**
- **Master's** (M.S., M.Ed., M.A.): Core practitioner credential; 30-36 credit hours; portfolio-based completion
- **Ed.D.** (Doctorate of Education): Applied/professional doctorate; practitioner-researchers; policy and leadership emphasis
- **Ph.D.**: Research doctorate; original empirical contribution; academic careers and research labs

## 3. The Textbook Canon — Tier 1 (Universal Core Texts)

These texts appear on syllabi across virtually every graduate ID program in the US.

### *The Systematic Design of Instruction* — Dick, Carey & Carey (9th ed., 2015)
**Used at**: FSU, Indiana, Penn State, UCF, Syracuse, and most ID programs
**What it covers**: The Dick & Carey model (10-step systems approach); needs assessment; instructional analysis; learner and context analysis; performance objectives; criterion-referenced testing; instructional strategy; formative and summative evaluation
**Why it matters**: Considered the definitive single text on the ID process. A common graduate textbook for the core foundations course. The Dick & Carey model is the most widely taught systematic ID model globally.
**Key concept**: The model is a recursive system — every component feeds back into others; evaluation informs revision at every stage.

### *Instructional Design* — Smith & Ragan (3rd ed., 2004)
**Used at**: Indiana, Penn State, Purdue, UCF, and widely
**What it covers**: Learning theory foundations (behaviorism, cognitivism, constructivism) applied to ID; declarative and procedural knowledge design; attitude learning; psychomotor design; comprehensive analysis-strategy-assessment-evaluation framework
**Why it matters**: Uniquely strong on designing instructional strategies for different learning types (Gagné's learning outcomes taxonomy applied systematically). Emphasizes the theoretical underpinnings of design decisions.
**Key concept**: Every design choice should be traceable to a theory of how people learn the specific type of content involved.

### *Designing Effective Instruction* — Morrison, Ross, Kalman & Kemp (7th ed., 2013)
**Used at**: Penn State, Purdue, Indiana, and broadly
**What it covers**: The Kemp/Morrison model — nine elements addressed in any order (flexible, non-linear approach); instructional problems; learner characteristics; task analysis; objectives; content sequencing; instructional strategies; evaluation; revision
**Why it matters**: The most flexible of the major models; emphasizes that ID is not a linear process. Good for practitioners in agile/iterative contexts.
**Key concept**: The oval model — all nine elements are interdependent and can be addressed in any sequence based on project context.

### *Principles of Instructional Design* — Gagné, Wager, Golas & Keller (5th ed., 2004)
**Used at**: FSU (home of Gagné's legacy), Indiana, Purdue, and broadly
**What it covers**: Gagné's conditions of learning (five learning outcomes: intellectual skills, verbal information, cognitive strategies, motor skills, attitudes); nine events of instruction; learning hierarchy analysis; instructional strategy design
**Why it matters**: Gagné's taxonomy is the theoretical foundation for most ID models. The nine events of instruction remain the most widely applied instructional sequencing framework in both academia and practice.
**Key concept**: Different types of learning outcomes require different instructional conditions — the type of learning determines the instructional design.

### *e-Learning and the Science of Instruction* — Clark & Mayer (5th ed., 2023)
**Used at**: Penn State, Purdue, Michigan State, UCF, and virtually all programs covering eLearning
**What it covers**: Twelve multimedia learning principles (Coherence, Signaling, Redundancy, Spatial Contiguity, Temporal Contiguity, Segmenting, Pre-training, Modality, Multimedia, Personalization, Voice, Image) with meta-analytic research evidence for each
**Why it matters**: The single most evidence-based guide to eLearning design. Every principle is grounded in cognitive load theory and dual-coding theory research. Required reading for any ID practitioner designing digital learning.
**Key concept**: The cognitive theory of multimedia learning — people learn more deeply from words and pictures together than from words alone, but only when design reduces extraneous cognitive load.

## 4. The Textbook Canon — Tier 2 (Widely Assigned Theory Texts)

### *Trends and Issues in Instructional Design and Technology* — Reiser & Dempsey (eds., 4th ed., 2018)
**Used at**: FSU, Indiana, Purdue, and most doctoral programs
**What it covers**: Historical roots of the field; major ID models; performance improvement; eLearning; distance education; knowledge management; informal learning; future trends
**Why it matters**: The field's primary survey text — introduces students to the breadth of ID and educational technology. Contains chapters by the field's major scholars including Gagné, Keller, Jonassen, Stolovitch, and Rossett.

### *Instructional-Design Theories and Models* (Vols. I–IV) — Reigeluth (ed.)
**Used at**: Indiana (Reigeluth's home institution), doctoral programs broadly
**Vol. I** (1983): Classic models — Gagné, Bruner, Ausubel, Merrill, Landa
**Vol. II** (1999): New paradigm — constructivist learning, self-regulated learning, situated learning, Merrill's ITT
**Vol. III** (2009): Learner-centered education — personalized integrated educational systems
**Vol. IV** (2017): Merging ID with learner-centered theory; non-instructional interventions; rapid prototyping
**Why it matters**: The definitive theoretical reference for the field. Doctoral students are expected to know these volumes. Reigeluth's Elaboration Theory (Vol. I) and learner-centered framework (Vol. III) are especially influential.

### *Ten Steps to Complex Learning* — van Merriënboer & Kirschner (3rd ed., 2018)
**Used at**: Purdue, Penn State, Indiana, and programs emphasizing complex cognitive skill training
**What it covers**: The 4C/ID model (Four-Component Instructional Design) — learning tasks, supportive information, procedural information, part-task practice; whole-task approach to complex skill training
**Why it matters**: The gold standard for designing instruction for complex professional competencies (medicine, engineering, management). Addresses the critical failure of traditional ID: fragmented instruction that doesn't transfer to complex real-world performance.
**Key concept**: Start with authentic whole tasks, not isolated component skills. Complexity is managed through scaffolding and variability, not simplification.

### *Understanding by Design* — Wiggins & McTighe (2nd ed., 2005)
**Used at**: Education programs broadly; K-12 emphasis but widely applied
**What it covers**: Backward design — start with desired results (Stage 1), determine acceptable evidence (Stage 2), then plan learning experiences (Stage 3)
**Why it matters**: The most influential curriculum design framework in K-12 education; increasingly applied in higher education and corporate learning. Prioritizes understanding over coverage-oriented instruction — the distinction that defines the backward design approach.
**Key concept**: Begin with the end in mind. Design backward from the evidence you'll accept that learning occurred, not forward from content to be covered.

### *The ID CaseBook* — Ertmer, Quinn & Glazewski (5th ed., 2019)
**Used at**: Widely used in ID foundations courses at Purdue (Ertmer's home), Penn State, and others
**What it covers**: 26 realistic open-ended ID case studies across K-12, post-secondary, and corporate contexts; case-based reasoning methodology
**Why it matters**: Bridges theory and practice through authentic ID scenarios. Students develop problem-solving judgment, not just procedural knowledge. Used in foundations courses to develop the "design judgment" that distinguishes expert IDs.

### *Foundations of Learning and Instructional Design Technology* — West (ed., 2018, Open Textbook)
**Used at**: Utah State and many programs using open educational resources
**What it covers**: History of the field; learning theories; design models; technology and media; emerging directions; open access
**Why it matters**: Winner of 2018 AECT Outstanding Book Award; freely available; comprehensive introduction to the field for new graduate students. Faculty across the country assign it as a cost-free alternative to expensive texts.

### *Creating Significant Learning Experiences* — Fink (2nd ed., 2013)
**Used at**: Higher education faculty development; graduate education programs
**What it covers**: Taxonomy of significant learning (foundational knowledge, application, integration, human dimension, caring, learning how to learn); backward design for course development; active learning strategies
**Why it matters**: Widely used in faculty development programs and graduate ID courses focused on higher education. Fink's taxonomy extends Bloom's by including affective and metacognitive dimensions.

## 5. The Textbook Canon — Tier 3 (Specialized & Advanced Graduate Texts)

### Performance Consulting & HPT

***Performance Consulting* — Robinson, Robinson, Phillips & Phillips (3rd ed., 2015)**
The definitive HPT text for moving from training order-taker to performance consultant. Required in performance improvement courses at Indiana and FSU.

***Analyzing Performance Problems* — Mager & Pipe (3rd ed., 1997)**
Classic diagnostic flowchart for determining whether training is the right solution. Still universally taught despite age.

***Handbook of Human Performance Technology* — Pershing (ed., 3rd ed., 2006)**
The HPT field's comprehensive reference. Covers the full range of performance interventions beyond training.

***Beyond Training Ain't Performance* — Stolovitch & Keeps (2004)**
Accessible argument for performance-centered design. Common supplemental reading in HPT/ID courses.

### Learning Theory & Cognitive Science

***How People Learn I & II* — National Academies (2000, 2018)**
Synthesizes cognitive science research on learning. Used as foundational reading in learning sciences courses at Vanderbilt, Michigan State, and others.

***Efficiency in Learning* — Clark, Nguyen & Sweller (2005)**
Applies Cognitive Load Theory to instructional design. Core reading in courses on multimedia and eLearning design.

***The Cambridge Handbook of the Learning Sciences* — Sawyer (ed., 2nd ed., 2014)**
Academic handbook covering all major learning sciences research areas — constructivism, PBL, CSCL, knowledge transfer, expertise. Used in doctoral seminars.

***Learning Theories: An Educational Perspective* — Schunk (7th ed., 2019)**
The most comprehensive single text on learning theory for educators. Covers behaviorism, social cognitive theory, information processing, constructivism, motivation. Common in learning theory survey courses.

### Assessment & Evaluation

***Practical Assessment, Research, and Evaluation* (journal + texts)**
Widely used in evaluation courses. Accessible research on formative and summative assessment.

***Kirkpatrick's Four Levels of Training Evaluation* — J. & W. Kirkpatrick (2016)**
The evaluation canon. Required in virtually every ID and CLO program.

***The Handbook of Training Evaluation and Measurement Methods* — Phillips (4th ed., 1997)**
The ROI methodology text. Required in graduate evaluation courses and CLO programs.

### Online & Distance Learning

***eLearning in the 21st Century* — Garrison (3rd ed., 2017)**
Framework for e-learning in higher education; Community of Inquiry model. Used in online learning design courses.

***Building Online Learning Communities* — Palloff & Pratt (2nd ed., 2007)**
Practical strategies for virtual classroom community building. Common in online course design courses.

***Distance Education: A Systems View* — Moore & Kearsley (3rd ed., 2011)**
Foundational text in distance education theory. Transactional distance theory. Used in distance/online learning courses.

### Emerging Areas

***Learning in a Digital Age* — various Jossey-Bass / ATD Press titles**
Increasingly assigned for topics like microlearning, mobile learning, social learning, and performance support.

***Design Thinking for Training and Development* — Boller & Fletcher (2020)**
Increasingly assigned as LXD and design thinking methodologies enter the ID curriculum.

**Culturally Inclusive Instructional Design* — Edmonds & Richardson (2020)**
Winner of AECT 2019 Outstanding Book Award. Addresses diversity, equity, and global design contexts. Increasingly required in foundations courses.

## 6. Major ID Theoretical Frameworks — Graduate-Level Application Notes

The model knows these frameworks. The notes below reflect how each is applied at the graduate practitioner level — what's tested, what's often missed, and where practitioners over-apply or under-apply the model.

| Framework | Graduate/Practitioner Application Note |
|---|---|
| **Gagné's Conditions of Learning / Nine Events** | Audit instructional sequences against Nine Events; check that conditions match the *type* of learning outcome (intellectual skill vs. attitude vs. motor) — not a one-size-fits-all template |
| **Bloom's Revised Taxonomy** | Write objectives at the *correct* cognitive level × knowledge dimension (factual/conceptual/procedural/metacognitive); align assessment to objective level — don't test application when objective is analysis |
| **Merrill's First Principles** | Audit any design against all five; the most violated is Principle 1 (problem-centered) — courses that teach theory before authentic problems break the model's core premise |
| **Reigeluth's Elaboration Theory** | Sequencing: simple-to-complex with epitomizing overview first, *not* part-to-whole; directly challenges the "foundations first, advanced later" default |
| **van Merriënboer's 4C/ID** | For complex professional competencies: whole authentic tasks first, supportive info available (not pre-taught), procedural info just-in-time, part-task practice only for automatable sub-skills. The most common misapplication: fragmenting complex skills into isolated units |
| **Jonassen's CLEs** | Designers create environments and challenges, not instruction. The ID's job is to design the problem, the resources, and the social structure — not to deliver answers |
| **Keller's ARCS** | Motivational design audit: which of the four components is actually missing in this design? Most courses under-invest in Relevance (C) and Satisfaction (S). Design pre-learning to build motivation *before* content begins |
| **Ertmer & Newby's Learning Theory Comparison** | Match design orientation to content type: behavioral for procedural drill/compliance, cognitive for schema-building and information processing, constructivist for complex problem-solving. Pluralistic, not dogmatic |
| **Mezirow's Transformative Learning** | For attitudinal/perspective-change goals (DEI, leadership, culture change): design for disorienting dilemma → critical reflection → discourse with others. Cannot be achieved through information transfer alone |
| **Vygotsky's ZPD** | Scaffold in the zone between unaided and guided performance — not below it (boring) or above it (shutdown). Adaptive systems and coaching programs that miss this produce either disengagement or anxiety |

## 8. AECT & IBSTPI Standards — Academic Accreditation Context

### AECT (Association for Educational Communications and Technology)
**Role**: The primary academic professional organization for the ID/EdTech field; peer reviewed journals; annual conference; accreditation standards
**AECT Standards (2012)**: Five domains — Content Knowledge, Content Pedagogy, Learning Environments, Professional Knowledge and Skills, Research
**Graduate program alignment**: Many programs frame program outcomes around AECT standards; doctoral dissertations in the field are often published through AECT journals

### IBSTPI (International Board of Standards for Training, Performance, and Instruction)
**Role**: Defines competency standards for instructional designers, trainers, online instructors, and performance improvement practitioners
**ID Competencies (2012)**: 22 competencies across four domains — Professional Foundations, Planning and Analysis, Design and Development, Evaluation and Implementation
**Graduate application**: Penn State, Syracuse, and others explicitly align capstone portfolios to IBSTPI standards; used in program assessment and accreditation

## 10. CLO Strategy — The Academic & Executive Curriculum

### Strategic Learning Architecture
The CLO's primary strategic responsibility — designing the learning ecosystem that enables organizational capability:
- **Build/Buy/Borrow framework**: When to develop internally vs. acquire externally vs. curate from outside
- **Learning ecosystem mapping**: Formal + informal + social + performance support — all touchpoints designed intentionally
- **Skills architecture**: Defining the critical skills for organizational strategy; mapping learning to skill gaps at scale
- **Learning culture diagnosis**: Using Senge's dimensions of a learning organization to assess and develop culture

### Key CLO Texts & Required Reading

**_The Fifth Discipline_ — Peter Senge (1990/2006)**
The foundational text on learning organizations. Five disciplines: systems thinking, personal mastery, mental models, shared vision, team learning. Required reading in virtually every CLO program.

**_Organizational Learning II_ — Argyris & Schön (1996)**
Single-loop vs. double-loop learning; defensive routines; theories-in-use vs. espoused theories. Critical for CLOs designing cultures that learn from failure.

**_The Fearless Organization_ — Amy Edmondson (2018)**
Psychological safety as the foundation of organizational learning. Essential for CLOs building cultures where people surface problems and experiment without fear.

**_Good to Great_ — Jim Collins (2001)**
Business strategy classic; widely assigned in CLO leadership curricula for the Level 5 Leadership model and disciplined strategy.

**_The Six Disciplines of Breakthrough Learning_ — Pollock, Jefferson & Wick (3rd ed., 2015)**
6Ds framework for turning learning investments into performance outcomes. Critical for CLOs focused on transfer and business impact.

**_Measuring the Success of Learning Through Technology_ — Phillips, Phillips & Ray (2012)**
ROI methodology applied to technology-based learning; data collection instruments; reporting frameworks.

**_Winning with a Culture of Recognition_ — Mosley & Irvine (2012)**
Recognition and motivation as learning culture levers. Assigned in some CLO programs focused on engagement and learning culture.

### Talent Analytics for CLOs
Graduate-level CLO programs increasingly cover:
- **Learning data literacy**: xAPI data, LRS architecture, learning dashboards
- **Business-aligned metrics**: Not just completion rates — skill growth velocity, performance improvement correlation, retention impact, time-to-productivity for new hires
- **People analytics integration**: L&D data within broader HR analytics frameworks
- **Predictive analytics**: Using learning data to forecast performance, flight risk, and promotion readiness
- **ROI measurement**: Kirkpatrick Levels 3 & 4; Phillips Level 5 ROI; isolating L&D's contribution

### CLO as Strategic Business Partner
Academic programs emphasize that the modern CLO must be:
- **Fluent in business language**: Revenue, margin, EBITDA, headcount ratios — not just training hours and completion rates
- **A change agent**: Learning strategy is change management. Every major initiative requires a learning architecture.
- **A talent strategist**: Partnering with CHRO on succession, workforce planning, and critical skill development
- **A technology leader**: LMS/LXP strategy, AI adoption, data governance — the CLO owns the learning technology stack
- **A culture architect**: The visible champion of continuous learning; modeling intellectual curiosity at the executive level

## 12. Research Methodology in ID — Graduate Level

Graduate ID programs require research methodology literacy. Key approaches:

### Design-Based Research (DBR)
The field's primary native research methodology. Iterative cycles of design, implementation, analysis, and redesign in real-world settings. Produces both improved design artifacts AND theoretical understanding.
**Key reference**: Wang & Hannafin (2005) in *Educational Technology Research and Development*

### Mixed Methods Research
Combines quantitative (learning analytics, test scores, survey data) and qualitative (interview, observation, artifact analysis) methods. Used in program evaluation and dissertation research.

### Quasi-Experimental Design
Pre/post comparisons with and without control groups. Used for evaluating training effectiveness and comparing instructional approaches.

### Instructional Development Research (IDR)
Systematic inquiry into the development process itself — not just outcomes, but how effective designs are created. Used to refine models and processes.

### Usability Studies
Formative evaluation methodology for learning products. Think-aloud protocols, eye tracking, interaction data analysis.

### Key Statistical Concepts for ID Researchers
- Effect size (Cohen's d) — more meaningful than p-values for practical significance
- Meta-analysis — synthesizing findings across studies; Clark & Mayer cite meta-analytic evidence throughout
- Multilevel modeling — when analyzing learner data nested within courses or organizations

## 13. Connecting Academic Theory to Practitioner Work

### The Research-Practice Gap in ID
A persistent tension in the field: practitioners often don't read research; researchers often don't solve real practitioner problems. Bridging strategies:

**Translational reading**: When a practitioner encounters a design challenge, trace it to the relevant research tradition. Example: "Learners aren't retaining content" → Retrieval practice research (Roediger & Butler) → Design implication: add spaced low-stakes retrieval activities.

**Evidence-based design**: Every major design decision should be traceable to either empirical evidence or explicit theoretical reasoning. "We used worked examples because CLT research shows they reduce extraneous cognitive load for novice learners" is a defensible design decision. "The SME wanted it this way" is not.

**Theory as a diagnostic tool**: When a learning solution isn't working, academic theory provides a diagnostic framework. Engagement problems → ARCS model analysis. Transfer problems → 6Ds/transfer climate analysis. Cognitive overload → CLT principles review.

### How to Read an ID Research Article (Graduate Primer)
1. Read the abstract and conclusion first — determine relevance
2. Read the literature review for the theoretical framing
3. Examine the methodology — how was it studied? What are the limitations?
4. Evaluate the findings — effect sizes matter more than significance
5. Apply critically — does the context match yours? What are the boundary conditions?
