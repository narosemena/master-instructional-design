# Situational Leadership — Coaching Scaffold

## The Core Diagnostic Question

Before selecting a leadership style, identify: *"What is this person's development level for **this specific task**?"* Development level is task-specific, not person-specific — a veteran facilitator may be D1 on a new LMS rollout.

---

## Development Level Cues in L&D Contexts

| Level | What You Observe | Common L&D Example |
|-------|-----------------|-------------------|
| D1 — Enthusiastic Beginner | High motivation, low competence; asks lots of questions, may not know what they don't know | New ID hired from teaching; eager but unfamiliar with ADDIE |
| D2 — Disillusioned Learner | Growing competence, declining motivation; "this is harder than I thought" | IDs six months in who can build a course but keep hitting SME friction |
| D3 — Capable but Cautious | High competence, variable confidence; capable but won't act without reassurance | Senior ID who consistently delivers but hesitates to own client relationships |
| D4 — Self-Reliant Achiever | High competence, high commitment; can self-direct | Expert facilitator who needs minimal oversight and actively mentors others |

---

## Matching Style to Level — The L&D Manager's Cheat Sheet

| Style | Behaviors | When to Use |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| S1 — Directing | Tell, show, close supervision; one-way instruction | D1: they need clarity, not dialogue |
| S2 — Coaching | Still directive on *what*, but two-way on *how*; explain reasoning | D2: they need understanding + motivation recovery |
| S3 — Supporting | Collaborative, ask-don't-tell, encourage self-direction | D3: competence is there; build confidence |
| S4 — Delegating | Hand off ownership; available but not hovering | D4: get out of the way; assign stretch goals |

---

## Most Common L&D Manager Mistakes

1. **Defaulting to S2 for everyone** — Coaching feels collegial and safe, but D1 people need S1 (clear direction), and D4 people experience S2 as micromanagement
2. **Diagnosing the person, not the task** — "She's a D4" applied globally misses that even strong performers are D1 on new skills
3. **Style-flexibility as inconsistency** — Teams may read appropriate flexibility as favoritism; name the model explicitly when you shift styles
4. **Skipping S3** — Moving D2 → D4 without S3 leaves capable people without confidence scaffolding

---

## Coaching Question Sequence

When working with an L&D manager or CLO on a team member situation, ask in order:

1. "What's the specific task or responsibility we're talking about?"
2. "What does their performance tell you about their competence level for that task?"
3. "What does their behavior tell you about their motivation or confidence level?"
4. "Which quadrant does that put them in — D1, D2, D3, or D4?"
5. "What style have you been using? Is it a match?"

---

## When SLII Breaks Down

- **Matrixed orgs**: You don't have supervisory authority over SMEs — shift to influence-based S2/S3 behaviors without the authority assumption
- **Volunteer or external facilitators**: Relationship management, not supervision; S3/S4 is the ceiling
- **Remote and distributed teams**: Observational cues are harder to read; build explicit check-in rhythms to diagnose level before selecting style
- **Psychological safety low**: D2-style candor ("here's what's not working") requires trust; don't run S2 in a blame culture
