# Corporate Communications for L&D Leaders — Coaching Scaffold

## The Core Failure Pattern

L&D professionals routinely write communications that lead with activities ("We launched a new course on…") instead of outcomes ("Sales conversion improved 12% after…"). Executives don't fund activities — they fund results. Every communication should answer *"So what does this mean for the business?"* before they ask.

---

## The Pyramid Principle Applied to L&D Updates

Structure all executive communication bottom-up in your thinking, top-down in your writing:

1. **Lead with the answer** (BLUF: Bottom Line Up Front) — the result, recommendation, or ask
2. **Support with 3 key points** — evidence, rationale, or proof points
3. **Detail only if asked** — data, methodology, appendices on request

**L&D application — before/after:**

| Before (activity-led) | After (outcome-led) |
|----------------------|---------------------|
| "We completed 847 course completions in Q1" | "Q1 compliance risk dropped 34%; 847 employees certified ahead of the May deadline" |
| "We're requesting budget for a new LMS" | "Our current LMS is losing us 22% of learner starts to friction; a $40K investment recovers an estimated $180K in productivity" |

## Stakeholder Message Map

Before writing any communication, complete this 4-column table:

| Audience | What they care about | Key message for them | Best channel + timing |
|---------- |---------------------|---------------------|----------------------|
| C-Suite | ROI, risk, strategy | Business impact + investment return | Exec summary, monthly; 1 page max |
| People managers | Their team's performance | What's in it for their direct reports | Manager email + talking points |
| Learners | WIIFM, time, relevance | "This will help you do X faster/better" | Email + manager cascade + nudge |
| L&D sponsors | Milestone confidence | On-track signals + risk flags | Bi-weekly check-in |

## L&D Brand Voice Checklist (3 Questions)

Before publishing any learner-facing communication, ask:

1. **Does it sound like a legal notice or like a trusted colleague?** — eliminate passive voice, hedging, and compliance-speak
2. **Does it respect learners' time?** — subject line + first sentence must earn the open; don't bury the point
3. **Is the tone consistent with how we'd talk in a room together?** — formal ≠ professional; conversational ≠ unprofessional

## Presentation Structure for CLOs Reporting to Executives

1. **Situation** (1 slide): What was the business problem or opportunity?
2. **Intervention** (1 slide): What L&D did and why this approach
3. **Results** (1–2 slides): Quantified outcomes; Kirkpatrick L3/L4 where available
4. **So what** (1 slide): What this means for strategy or the next investment decision
5. **Ask** (1 slide): Decision needed, resource requested, or approval sought

*The mistake*: Most L&D decks spend 80% on the intervention (activities) and 5% on results. Flip the ratio.

---

## Common Communication Failure Modes

- **Jargon overload**: ADDIE, xAPI, SAM, ILT — none of these mean anything to executives; translate every acronym to a business concept
- **Vanity metrics**: Completions, seat hours, and satisfaction scores signal effort, not impact; pair every activity metric with a performance metric
- **Burying the ask**: Executives lose patience; put your request in paragraph one, not paragraph five
- **Over-explaining methodology**: Executives don't need to know how you built it — they need to know what changed as a result
