# Stakeholder Communication Architecture

Complete language frameworks for the conversations that determine whether
learning projects succeed or get rebuilt. Every sequence below is verbatim
— use it, adapt the specifics, preserve the structure.

---

## Core Principle

Not all stakeholder conversations are closed the same way.

| Conversation Type | Closing Philosophy |
|---|---|
| **Scope change / resource decision** | Create urgency, name delay cost, get a decision in the room — project stalls without authorization |
| **Evaluation commitment** | Encourage genuine ownership, allow reflection, invite additional stakeholders — commitment quality determines execution |

A pressured yes on an evaluation commitment produces no follow-through
data. A stakeholder who leaves a scope change conversation without a
decision costs the team sprint capacity. Read which conversation you are
in before choosing your close.

## The Scope Change Conversation — Full Sequence

Applies when pilot or observation data requires a scope change conversation
with the project sponsor.

---

### Opening — The Finding

> *"As we continue to analyze the work ahead, we have found that through
> systematic observation of learners in the place of work, we must recommend
> we reevaluate the scope of this learning intervention. In observations we
> could not find visible evidence that team members have the necessary
> mastery of the non-system side of the work."*

**Key language principles:**
- "As we continue to analyze" — positions finding as diligence, not failure
- "Systematic observation" — grounds recommendation in evidence, not opinion
- "Could not confidently observe" — precise; absence of confirmation, not
  assertion of absence
- Preserves SME credibility throughout

---

### The Recommendation

> *"I am going to recommend we make a clean distinction in the skills needed
> to do this work and address these individually as goals for the learner to
> master, while at the same time showing how they come together in the
> moment of truth."*

---

### Opportunity + Risk Statement

> *"We recommend we move forward with this approach to make sure we diminish
> quality issues — errors in QC, missed target metrics, downstream problems
> with checked agent calls. And I want to make the risk perfectly visible:
> we are looking at quality issues that will only compound downstream. A
> poorly judged customer call is an agent who will not get coached, or could
> be put on a performance improvement plan unnecessarily. This approach lets
> us build quality into the process."*

**Key language principles:**
- Opportunity framed as business/operational outcome, not learner experience
- Risk named with a specific, human, recognizable consequence
- PIP reference activates fairness and legal awareness without stating it
- "Build quality into the process" borrows continuous improvement language

---

### The Close — Decision Request

> *"Here is how this changes our approach — I want to make this clear and
> make sure I am getting your honest buy-in to continue. We will need more
> time and resources assigned to this new vertical. Our team capacity is
> limited and we work in sprints. We need to start the process properly with
> an intake in the context of the judging skills. This means we will run
> these two verticals concurrently at times, which means more time with
> SMEs and more review and approval cycles. I am confident we have given
> you all the information in a way that helps you make a decision about our
> recommendation. If we have not, we are happy to provide additional details
> or answer any questions right now. Are you ok with us proceeding this
> way?"*

---

### If Stakeholder Says "Let Me Think About It"

> *"Certainly. I understand you may need some time — please let me know at
> any point if you need additional information from us. In the meantime we
> will put this work back in our backlog and move to different priorities
> while a decision is made. With that in mind, in the spirit of
> transparency, I cannot guarantee we would be able to pick up this project
> right away, especially since we may assign other work in our backlog to
> make use of my team's capacity. I want you to rest assured we will give
> this work the priority it requires."*

**Why this works:**
- Backlog language makes delay cost tangible without issuing a threat
- Team capacity reframe: the project needs the team, not the reverse
- Closes on commitment and professionalism

---

### If Decision-Maker Is Not in the Room

> *"Absolutely — how could we help you? Would it be appropriate to set a
> meeting with the executive and us? What other information do you need
> from us?"*

- Offer to participate in escalation meeting — prevents telephone game
- Apply backlog urgency language to create timeline pressure on scheduling

## The Pre-Launch Gap Conversation

Applies when change management support is absent — no Awareness campaign,
no Desire-building with managers, no Reinforcement infrastructure in the
performance environment.

**The principle:**
No amount or quality of training can solve for Awareness, Desire, or
Reinforcement. The learning leader has a professional obligation to name
this gap to the sponsor before launch — not after the training fails.

**What moves sponsors — the two-sentence version:**

> *"What you will see in 90 days is an increase in attrition. Such a level
> of dissatisfaction that the tribal knowledge will walk out the door — and
> the ones that stay will do so on pure survivability needs."*

**Why this works:**
- "Tribal knowledge walks out the door" — names irreplaceable institutional
  knowledge loss that isn't documented anywhere
- "Pure survivability needs" — remaining employees are not committed, they
  are trapped; that is a quality floor, not a quality standard
- Moves the conversation from learning outcomes → talent retention →
  operational continuity → compliance risk in two sentences
- Zero L&D jargon

## The Evaluation Conversation

### Opening Move

> *"Let's talk about how we would measure success for this learning
> intervention. There are multiple levels and ways we can evaluate — let me
> present it in two layers. First: what level of evaluation is required from
> the learner about the course itself? Is it enough to capture whether they
> liked the course and found it relevant? Do we need auditable proof of
> learning such as a quiz or test?"*

**Purpose:** Gets the customer talking before presenting options. Surfaces
their instinctive sense of accountability without Kirkpatrick jargon.

---

### Reading the Customer's First Reaction

| Customer Response | Signal | Next Move |
|---|---|---|
| "Nothing really — I thought that was always how it was done" | Assumption-driven, no real need | Reframe evaluation around actual purpose |
| "Because we have to present this to a government auditor" | Compliance requirement | Define minimum viable documentation standard |
| Connects evaluation to a business outcome unprompted | Sophisticated customer | Move directly to Kirkpatrick architecture conversation |
| Goes quiet or gives vague answer | Hasn't thought about it | Begin customer education sequence |

**The dominant pattern — the uninformed yes:**
Most customers instinctively say yes to evaluation options not because they
understand the implications but because they fear missing something. This
is FOMO driving a design decision, not informed consent.

**The diagnostic follow-up:**
> *"Why do you think a graded quiz is needed? What purpose will being able
> to provide a key executive with a pass/fail report serve you?"*

---

### The Kirkpatrick Teaching Sequence

**Opening move — levity before content:**
> *"Can I walk you through the normal logic behind evaluation strategy?
> I assure you there is a method to our madness."*

**Level 1 — Reaction:**
Do learners feel the content was relevant to their job? Do they feel
prepared to apply it today?

**Level 2 — Knowledge transfer:**
An assessment that measures whether key concepts, ideas, and processes
stayed with the learner — not memorization, but retention of what they
need to perform.

**Level 3 — Application:**
Observation on the job — real time or at 30, 60, 90 days. Are we seeing
the behavior change? Are learners applying what they learned?

**Level 4 — Return on investment:**
What evidence exists that training moved the needle? What is the measurable
business impact?

**Guidance layer close:**
> *"Your choice is influenced by multiple factors — what the team needs,
> what auditors or regulators require, what the Chief Quality Officer
> expects. We may not have all those answers today. But we can map the
> next steps from here."*

---

### Level 4 Timing Constraint — Critical Decision Rule

Level 4 evaluation is not primarily a capability constraint — it is a
**timing constraint.**

To measure business impact, measurement infrastructure must be established
before the project begins — baseline metrics identified, data collection
mechanisms in place, business stakeholders committed to tracking outcomes.

| Condition | Level 4 Viability |
|---|---|
| L&D involved at project inception | Viable — recommend when business stakes justify it |
| L&D brought in mid-flight | Not viable — measurement posts cannot be painted retroactively |

This constraint belongs in the design document as an explicit flag —
not discovered at the evaluation conversation.

---

### Evaluation Recommendation — Closing Philosophy

> *"Here is how this changes our approach — I want to make sure I am getting
> your honest buy-in to continue. We will need more time and resources
> assigned..."*

**The commitment quality principle:**
A customer who says yes under closing pressure but doesn't fully own the
decision will deprioritize 30-60-90 touchpoints when the sprint fills up.

When to encourage a decision in the room: when the customer signals they
have the authority and information to decide.

When to allow reflection: when they hesitate — that hesitation carries
information. Do not override it with a closing technique.

## Scope Creep — Mid-Sprint Change Request Language

### Designer to Learning Leader — The Escalation Briefing

Escalation is not a memo. It is a written document plus a conversation.

**Four elements the designer brings:**
1. **Nature of the request** — what exactly is being asked and by whom
2. **Reason for the request** — what is driving it from the SME's perspective
3. **Workload impact** — specific effect on timeline, sprint capacity,
   and downstream deliverables
4. **Execution plan** — the designer's recommended path forward

**Format principle:** Written documentation enables recall. The conversation
provides context the document cannot fully capture. Both are required.

---

### Designer to SME — The Holding Response

In the window between receiving a change request and the learning leader
executing the formal response:

**Tone:** Transparency and partnership. The SME should feel heard and
informed — not managed or deflected.

**What to convey:**
- Acknowledge receipt explicitly
- Signal the change is being taken seriously
- Communicate next steps and why the request requires process
- Make no commitment to executing the work
- Commit to treating the request with the respect it deserves

---

### The Escalation Urgency Language

When the decision-maker delays and the team needs to move:

> *"In the meantime we will put this work back in our backlog and move to
> different priorities while a decision is made. With that in mind, in the
> spirit of transparency, I cannot guarantee we would be able to pick up
> this project right away, especially since we may assign other work in our
> backlog to make use of my team's capacity."*

This makes delay cost tangible without issuing a threat. The project needs
the team — not the reverse.
