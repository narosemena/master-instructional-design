# Stakeholder Communication — Scope Change, Evaluation, and Escalation

**Load when:** Scope change conversation is needed, evaluation commitment discussion is required, a discovery finding requires a sponsor-level conversation, or delay handling and escalation language is needed.

---

## The Scope Change Conversation — Full Sequence

This sequence applies when pilot or observation data requires a scope change conversation with the project sponsor. The most common trigger: verification reveals a missing skill that was assumed to be present, requiring a new design vertical.

---

### Opening — The Finding

> *"As we continue to analyze the work ahead, we have found that through systematic observation of learners in the place of work, we must recommend we reevaluate the scope of this learning intervention. In observations we could not find visible evidence that team members have the necessary mastery of the non-system side of the work."*

**Key language principles:**
- "As we continue to analyze" — positions finding as diligence, not failure
- "Systematic observation" — grounds recommendation in evidence, not opinion
- "Could not confidently observe" — precise; absence of confirmation, not assertion of absence
- Preserves SME credibility — the finding reflects what observation revealed, not what the SME got wrong

---

### The Recommendation

> *"I am going to recommend we make a clean distinction in the skills needed to do this work and address these individually as goals for the learner to master, while at the same time showing how they come together in the moment of truth."*

---

### Opportunity + Risk Statement

> *"We recommend we move forward with this approach to make sure we diminish quality issues — errors in QC, missed target metrics, downstream problems with checked agent calls. And I want to make the risk perfectly visible: we are looking at quality issues that will only compound downstream. A poorly judged customer call is an agent who will not get coached, or could be put on a performance improvement plan unnecessarily. This approach lets us build quality into the process."*

**Key language principles:**
- Opportunity framed as business/operational outcome, not learner experience
- Risk named with a specific, human, recognizable consequence
- PIP reference activates fairness and legal awareness without stating it explicitly
- "Build quality into the process" borrows continuous improvement language

---

### The Close — Decision Request

> *"Here is how this changes our approach — I want to make this clear and make sure I am getting your honest buy-in to continue. We will need more time and resources assigned to this new vertical. Our team capacity is limited and we work in sprints. We need to start the process properly with an intake in the context of the judging skills. This means we will run these two verticals concurrently at times, which means more time with SMEs and more review and approval cycles. I am confident we have given you all the information in a way that helps you make a decision about our recommendation. If we have not, we are happy to provide additional details or answer any questions right now. Are you ok with us proceeding this way?"*

---

### If Stakeholder Says "Let Me Think About It"

> *"Certainly. I understand you may need some time — please let me know at any point if you need additional information from us. In the meantime we will put this work back in our backlog and move to different priorities while a decision is made. With that in mind, in the spirit of transparency, I cannot guarantee we would be able to pick up this project right away, especially since we may assign other work in our backlog to make use of my team's capacity. I want you to rest assured we will give this work the priority it requires."*

**Why this works:**
- Backlog language makes delay cost tangible without issuing a threat
- Team capacity reframe: the project needs the team, not the reverse
- Closes on commitment and professionalism
- Does not chase the decision — creates natural urgency through operational reality

---

### If Decision-Maker Is Not in the Room

> *"Absolutely — how could we help you? Would it be appropriate to set a meeting with the executive and us? What other information do you need from us?"*

- Offer to participate in escalation meeting — prevents telephone game
- Apply backlog urgency language to create timeline pressure on scheduling the follow-up

## Two Closing Philosophies — A Critical Distinction

Junior IDs flatten all stakeholder conversations into one closing approach. The correct approach depends entirely on the type of conversation.

| Conversation Type | Closing Philosophy | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **Scope change / resource decision** | Create urgency, name delay cost, get a decision in the room | Project stalls without authorization; delay has direct operational cost |
| **Evaluation commitment / learning decision** | Encourage genuine ownership, allow reflection, invite additional stakeholders | Commitment quality determines execution; a pressured yes produces no follow-through data |

**The evaluation commitment principle:**
A customer who says yes under closing pressure but doesn't fully own the decision will deprioritize 30-60-90 touchpoints when the sprint fills up. A customer who sits with the recommendation, brings in the right stakeholders, and returns with genuine commitment will execute the evaluation plan.

The quality of the commitment matters more than the speed of the yes. This is not selling. It is building the conditions for evaluation success before launch.

**When to encourage a decision in the room:**
When the customer signals they have the authority and the information to decide — invite it. When they hesitate — that hesitation carries information. Do not override it with a closing technique.

## Escalation Language — Scope Creep Scenarios

When a scope change arrives mid-sprint and requires escalation to the learning leader:

**The designer's briefing to the learning leader contains four elements:**
1. Nature of the request — what exactly is being asked and by whom
2. Reason for the request — what is driving it from the SME's perspective
3. Workload impact — specific effect on timeline, sprint capacity, and downstream deliverables
4. Execution plan — the designer's recommended path forward

**The holding response to the SME (sent before the learning leader executes the formal response):**
- Acknowledges receipt explicitly
- Signals the change is being taken seriously
- Communicates next steps and why the request requires process
- Makes no commitment to executing the work
- Commits to treating the request with the respect it deserves

Tone: Transparency and partnership. The SME should feel heard and informed — not managed or deflected.
