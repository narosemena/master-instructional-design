## 1. Evaluation Philosophy — Start with the End

**The Kirkpatricks' new world model works backwards.** Don't plan evaluation after launch — design it alongside the learning solution from day one.

The sequence: Define Level 4 success first → determine what Level 3 behaviors produce that result → design Level 2 assessments that confirm those behaviors are possible → collect Level 1 data to improve the experience.

**Why most L&D evaluation fails:**
- L1 data is collected but never used to improve anything
- L2 assessments are knowledge checks, not skill demonstrations
- L3 is never measured because no one set it up before launch
- L4 metrics were never agreed on with the business sponsor

**The contract with the sponsor:** Before designing anything, get explicit agreement on: "In six months, if this worked, what will be different in the business?" That answer is your Level 4 metric. If the sponsor can't answer it, the project isn't scoped correctly yet.

## 2. The Evaluation Planning Conversation

Use these questions with sponsors at project kickoff to establish the evaluation contract:

**Level 4 — Business Results**
1. "What business problem prompted this request? How are you measuring it today?"
2. "If this learning program is completely successful, what number moves — and by how much?"
3. "Who in the organization tracks that metric? Can we get access to that data?"
4. "What's the realistic timeframe to see a behavior change reflected in business results?"

**Level 3 — Behavior Transfer**
5. "What will people be doing differently on the job six months from now if this worked?"
6. "Who manages the learners? Are managers willing to observe and report on behavior change?"
7. "What does the work environment look like — does it support the new behavior, or will people face barriers?"

**Level 2 — Learning**
8. "Do you want a formal pre/post assessment, or is scenario-based performance demonstration sufficient?"
9. "Is there a passing threshold requirement (compliance, certification)?"

**Level 1 — Reaction**
10. "What's your organization's standard for learner feedback? Any existing survey format I should align to?"

## 4. Level 1 — Reaction: Survey Design & Standards

### What a Good L1 Survey Measures
L1 measures learner reaction — not learning. Four dimensions (Kirkpatrick):
- **Relevance**: Was this relevant to my job?
- **Engagement**: Was the experience engaging and worthwhile?
- **Customer Satisfaction**: Would I recommend this to a colleague?
- **Intent to Apply**: Do I intend to apply what I learned?

Note: L1 alone does NOT predict learning or transfer. Use it to improve experience quality, not to prove impact.

### Standard L1 Survey Template

*5-point scale: Strongly Disagree → Strongly Agree (or 1–5 rating)*

**Relevance**
1. The content of this [course/session] was relevant to my current role.
2. The learning objectives were aligned to real challenges I face on the job.

**Engagement**
3. The learning experience held my attention throughout.
4. The examples and scenarios were realistic and applicable to my work.
5. The format and activities were appropriate for this content.

**Quality**
6. The content was well-organized and easy to follow.
7. The technical quality (audio, visuals, navigation) met my expectations.

**Intent to Apply**
8. I intend to apply what I learned in my role within the next 30 days.
9. I could describe to a colleague what I learned and how to apply it.

**Open-ended questions (essential):**
10. What was most valuable about this learning experience?
11. What would you change to improve this learning experience?
12. What support do you need to apply what you learned?

**Net Promoter Score (optional but powerful):**
"How likely are you to recommend this learning experience to a colleague? (0–10)"
NPS = % Promoters (9–10) minus % Detractors (0–6)

### L1 Benchmark Standards
- Relevance and engagement items: target ≥ 4.0/5.0 average
- Intent to apply: target ≥ 4.0/5.0
- Overall NPS: target ≥ +30 (excellent > +50)
- Response rate: target ≥ 60% (below 40% = data is unreliable)

### What to Do With L1 Data
- Review open-ended comments for patterns — not individual complaints
- Track trends over cohorts and over time — one low score means nothing; a pattern means something
- Share findings with SMEs and sponsors — they need to know if their content isn't landing
- Never suppress low L1 scores — they are diagnostic, not evaluative of the ID's performance

## 5. Level 2 — Learning: Assessment Design

### Assessment Design Principles
- Assessments must match the objective — if the objective is "apply," don't test recall
- Assessment items should be scenario-based wherever possible — not definition questions
- Feedback is part of the assessment — instructional feedback on incorrect answers is learning, not punishment
- Pre-assessment before instruction establishes baseline and enables gain scores

### Assessment Item Quality Checklist
For each multiple-choice question:
- [ ] Stem is scenario-based (describes a realistic situation), not a factual recall question
- [ ] All four options are plausible to someone who hasn't mastered the content
- [ ] One correct answer that a top performer would choose
- [ ] Distractors represent realistic errors or misconceptions (not obviously wrong)
- [ ] Feedback on incorrect choices explains the error and redirects, not just "incorrect"
- [ ] Feedback on correct choice explains why it's correct, reinforces the principle
- [ ] Reading level appropriate for the audience
- [ ] No double negatives, no "all of the above," no "none of the above"

### Pre/Post Assessment Design
**Pre-assessment purpose**: Establish baseline knowledge; personalize learning path (skip content learners already know); calculate learning gain.

**Post-assessment purpose**: Confirm achievement of objectives; meet compliance/certification requirements; generate L2 data.

**Gain score calculation**: Post-score minus pre-score = learning gain. A large gain is good evidence of L2 impact; a small gain with high pre-score may indicate the content was too basic.

### Alternative L2 Methods
| Method | Best For | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Multiple-choice quiz | Knowledge verification; compliance | Weakest for complex skills — combine with scenario |
| Branching scenario simulation | Decision-making; soft skills | Richer evidence of application capability |
| Performance demonstration | Motor skills; software tasks | Requires observer; most valid for complex skills |
| Role play (observed + scored) | Interpersonal skills; sales; coaching | Requires trained observer with rubric |
| Case study analysis | Analytical skills; diagnosis | Score against rubric — not just right/wrong |
| Portfolio / work product | Complex professional skills | Review against rubric; time-intensive |

## 6. Level 3 — Behavior: Transfer Measurement

L3 is the most neglected Kirkpatrick level — and the most predictive of L4 impact. It requires pre-planning and manager involvement. Design it before launch, not after.

### L3 Data Collection Methods

**Manager Observation Checklist**
The most direct measure of behavior transfer. Requires:
1. Designing the checklist during course design (not after launch)
2. Briefing managers before the course launches (what to look for, how to use the checklist)
3. Collecting data at 30, 60, and 90 days post-learning
4. Aggregating results across the population, not just individual reports

**Manager Observation Checklist Template:**

---

*Complete for each direct report who completed the program. Observe over 2–4 weeks.*

**Direct Report Name**: _______________ **Manager Name**: _______________ **Date**: _______________

| [Specific behavior from objective 2] | | | | | |
| [Specific behavior from objective 3] | | | | | |

**Overall transfer rating**: ☐ Significant improvement ☐ Some improvement ☐ No change ☐ Performance declined

**What has supported application?**:

**What has gotten in the way?**:

**What additional support would help?**:

---

**Learner Follow-Up Survey (30/60/90 Day)**
Shorter than the L1 survey; focused on application.

1. I have had opportunities to apply what I learned. (Y/N — if N, why not?)
2. I am applying what I learned in my day-to-day work. (1–5 scale)
3. The skills I learned have improved my performance in [specific area]. (1–5 scale)
4. What has made it easier to apply what you learned?
5. What has made it harder to apply what you learned?
6. What additional support would help you apply what you learned?

**Action Learning / Application Challenge**
Assign a specific on-the-job challenge to complete within 30 days. Learners report back:
- What did you do?
- What happened?
- What would you do differently?

This is the most powerful L3 tool — it creates application, not just measures it.

**Transfer Climate Assessment**
Before launch, assess whether the environment supports transfer:
- Do managers support and encourage the new behavior?
- Are there environmental barriers (no time, no tools, no incentive)?
- Are there incentives aligned to the new behavior?

If the transfer climate is poor, training will not produce L3 results regardless of quality. Escalate to the sponsor — this is a management and environment problem, not a training problem.

## 7. Level 4 — Results: Business Impact

### Identifying the Right L4 Metric
Not all training has a measurable L4. Be honest about this:

| Training Type | Likely L4 Metric | Data Source |
|---|---|---|
| Compliance training | Incident rate; audit findings; regulatory violations | Compliance / Legal |
| Sales training | Revenue per rep; close rate; pipeline velocity | CRM / Sales ops |
| Customer service | CSAT; resolution rate; escalation rate; handle time | Service platform |
| Leadership development | Employee engagement; retention; team performance scores | HRIS / Engagement survey |
| Safety training | Incident rate; near-miss reports; lost time injuries | Safety database |
| Onboarding | Time-to-productivity; 90-day retention; 90-day performance rating | HRIS / Manager rating |
| Technical skills | Error rate; quality scores; rework rate | Operations / Quality |

### Isolating Training's Contribution
This is the methodologically difficult part. Options from most to least rigorous:

1. **Control group**: Some employees get training, some don't; compare metrics (requires ethical consideration and organizational support)
2. **Trend analysis**: Compare metric before vs. after training, accounting for other changes in the business
3. **Forecasting**: What would the metric have been without training, based on prior trend? What was the actual result?
4. **Expert estimation**: Ask managers and participants to estimate what percentage of improvement is attributable to training (Phillips method) — less rigorous but practical
5. **Attribution disclosure**: Report the improvement and disclose that attribution is uncertain; recommend further study

**Honest L4 reporting note**: In most L&D contexts, Level 4 isolation is imprecise. Report what you measured, how you attempted to isolate training's contribution, and what the confidence level is. Overclaiming undermines credibility; transparent uncertainty builds it.

## 8. Level 5 — ROI: Phillips Methodology

ROI is warranted only for high-investment, high-stakes programs where executive sponsors need financial justification. Don't calculate ROI for every course.

### When to Calculate ROI
- Programs costing > $50,000 to develop or deliver
- Programs addressing a business problem with a clear dollar cost (e.g., turnover, errors, safety incidents)
- When the sponsor has explicitly asked for ROI
- Strategic programs where continued funding depends on demonstrating value

### The ROI Formula
```
ROI% = ((Benefits - Costs) / Costs) × 100

Where:
Benefits = monetary value of program results (L4)
Costs = fully-loaded program costs (development + delivery + learner time)
```

**Example:**
- Safety training program cost (fully loaded): $85,000
- Reduction in workplace incidents (3 fewer incidents × $45,000 average cost each): $135,000
- ROI = (($135,000 - $85,000) / $85,000) × 100 = **59% ROI**

### Fully-Loaded Cost Calculation
Include all costs — not just vendor fees:

| Cost Category | How to Calculate |
|---|---|
| Design & development | Hours × hourly rate for all L&D staff |
| SME time | Hours × their hourly loaded rate |
| Technology / licensing | Authoring tool, LMS, media assets |
| Facilitation | Facilitator time × hourly rate × number of sessions |
| Learner time | Average hours in program × average hourly salary × number of participants |
| Facilities / travel (if ILT) | Actual costs |
| Translation / localization | Vendor costs |
| Ongoing administration | Maintenance and re-runs over the program lifecycle |

**Learner time is usually the largest cost** and is almost always omitted. A 2-hour course for 500 employees at $35/hour average fully-loaded cost = $35,000 in learner time alone.

### Converting Benefits to Dollar Values
Common conversion approaches:
- **Direct output increase**: (Additional units × value per unit)
- **Cost reduction**: (Incidents reduced × average incident cost)
- **Time savings**: (Hours saved × hourly rate × volume)
- **Turnover reduction**: (Employees retained × cost to replace: typically 50–200% of annual salary)
- **Error reduction**: (Errors reduced × cost to correct per error)

When a direct dollar value isn't available, use expert estimation: ask two or three senior stakeholders to estimate the dollar value of one unit improvement in the metric. Take the conservative estimate.

## 9. Learning Analytics & xAPI Measurement

### What xAPI Enables Beyond SCORM

xAPI unlocks behavioral data that SCORM cannot capture:

| SCORM Tracks | xAPI Also Tracks |
|---|---|
| Completed / Not completed | Video watched to 73% and replayed section 3 |
| Score: 85% | Path through branching scenario; which choices were made |
| Time in course: 47 min | Specific resources searched for; knowledge base articles accessed |
| | On-the-job application challenge completed and verified |
| | Manager confirmed behavior transfer (via mobile check-in) |

### Key xAPI Statements for L&D Programs

**During learning:**
- `[Learner] attempted [activity]`
- `[Learner] answered [question] with [response] — correct/incorrect`
- `[Learner] completed [module] with score [X]`
- `[Learner] watched [video] to [percentage]`
- `[Learner] searched for [term] in knowledge base`

**Post-learning transfer:**
- `[Learner] completed [application challenge]`
- `[Manager] observed [learner] demonstrating [behavior]`
- `[Learner] self-reported applying [skill] on [date]`

### Learning Dashboard Design — Key Metrics to Surface

**Program health (L1/L2):**
- Enrollment and completion rates by department/cohort
- Average L1 scores (relevance, engagement, intent to apply)
- L2 pass rates; average score; attempt distribution
- Drop-off points in eLearning (where are learners quitting?)

**Transfer evidence (L3):**
- % of learners reporting application at 30/60/90 days
- Manager observation scores by behavior by cohort
- Completion rate of application challenge

**Business impact (L4):**
- Target metric trend line vs. program launch date
- Cohorts who completed training vs. those who haven't — metric comparison

**Operational efficiency:**
- Development hours per finished hour of learning (benchmark: 40–100:1 for eLearning)
- Cost per learner
- Time-to-competency for new hires

- NPS: [+X]

**Key Insights & Recommendations**
1. [What's working — specific evidence]
2. [What needs improvement — with proposed action]
3. [Recommended next steps]

### Executive Presentation Language
Translate L&D metrics into business language for C-suite reporting:

| Instead of... | Say... |
|---|---|
| "85% completion rate" | "850 of our 1,000 employees are now certified in [skill]" |
| "L1 score of 4.2/5.0" | "Learners rated the program highly relevant to their work" |
| "L2 pass rate: 92%" | "92% of participants demonstrated the skills in a realistic scenario" |
| "L3: 74% applying" | "3 in 4 managers report improved performance in [specific behavior] since the program launched" |
| "L4: error rate dropped 28%" | "Since launch, [X department] has reduced costly errors by 28% — saving an estimated $[Y] per quarter" |

### The Credibility Principle
Report accurately, even when results are disappointing. L&D teams that only report positive results lose credibility quickly. A program that didn't work is valuable data — it leads to a better version 2. Present negative findings with a diagnosis and a proposed fix, not an apology.
