# Hard-Change — Full Workflow

Learner already performs this task. Something must shift — system, process,
standard, or behavior. Design complexity depends entirely on which type of
change is required.

---

## The Four Hard-Change Scenarios

Hard-Change covers four distinct design problems that are not equally difficult:

| Scenario | Design Complexity |
|---|---|
| Adding a step to an existing process | Low — extend existing mental model |
| Performing the same task in a new system | Medium — transfer existing skill to new interface |
| Replacing a wrong behavior with a correct one | High — compete with established pattern |
| Unlearning a deeply habitual behavior | Highest — displace automated muscle memory with judgment-based decision making |

Unlearning is categorically harder than the other three because it requires
displacing behavior that is no longer consciously executed — it runs
automatically. The learner is not choosing the wrong behavior. They are not
choosing at all.

---

## The Three Resistance Types

Resistance to change in unlearning scenarios presents as three distinct
phenomena requiring different design responses. Treating all three as the
same problem is the most common Hard-Change design failure.

### Type 1 — Cognitive Resistance

**What it looks like:** Denial or rejection of new information. Learner
doesn't believe the new way is correct, better, or applicable to their
context.

**Root cause:** Epistemological — the learner hasn't accepted the premise.

**Design response:** Evidence and logic — but only when psychological safety
is present first. Data alone provokes defensiveness when it threatens
identity or status.

**The WIIFM reframe for veterans:**

Standard WIIFM fails in unlearning scenarios because veteran identity is
tied to procedural mastery. The reframe must move from procedure expertise
to outcome expertise.

*Example — contact center agents moving from 5-step to 3-step process:*

The veteran's status doesn't come from knowing the steps. It comes from
knowing what a good call resolution looks like. The new judgment-based
process doesn't threaten that expertise — it elevates it. A new hire can
memorize five steps. Knowing when and how to exercise judgment in the
consolidation step is what separates an expert from a technician.

The reframe belongs before the data, not after it. Data lands differently
when the veteran already sees themselves as the judgment expert.

---

### Type 2 — Emotional Resistance

**What it looks like:** Disengagement, complaining, flaw-finding, amplifying
the cost of change, refusing to accept the benefit of the new approach.

**Root cause:** Identity threat — the old behavior is tied to the learner's
sense of competence and status. More evidence makes this worse.

**Design response:** Co-creation with veterans. Psychological safety.
Acknowledgment of what the learner is being asked to give up.

**The genuine co-creation condition:**

- Veteran owns what the new behavior needs to achieve on the job
- Learning team owns how the learning experience is designed to get them there
- Boundaries are explicit from the first conversation
- Veteran input materially shapes the final design — not collected and ignored

**The performative co-creation trap:**

Veterans brought in as token gesture whose input is largely ignored become
the most vocal critics of the training — because they can point to exactly
where their expertise was dismissed. Performative co-creation produces worse
outcomes than no co-creation.

---

### Type 3 — Behavioral Resistance

**What it looks like:** Learner demonstrates knowledge of new behavior in
training but reverts to old behavior on the job. They can pass the
assessment and still not change.

**Root cause:** Transfer failure — the new behavior hasn't displaced the
habit loop in the performance environment. The old cues, rewards, and social
norms on the job still trigger the old behavior.

**Design response:** The performance environment must change alongside the
training. ADKAR framework — specifically Reinforcement — must be addressed
in the performance environment, not the training room.

**Scalable behavioral intervention:**

One-on-one coaching is correct but not scalable at volume. The learning team
must design reinforcement infrastructure into the intervention — manager
observation tools, job aids at the point of performance, peer accountability
structures, supervisor coaching guides.

## The ADKAR Ownership Model

ADKAR correctly identifies that behavior change requires five conditions:
Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement.

**Training addresses:** Knowledge and Ability only.

**Training cannot address:** Awareness, Desire, Reinforcement — these live
outside the training room in the performance environment and organizational
culture.

**The learning team's lane:**

The learning team owns Knowledge and Ability. That is the center of the
learning function. The discipline is staying in that lane while being honest
about what training cannot produce alone.

**The implementation gap:**

In most organizations the change management function and the learning
function operate separately. The learning team builds the training. Change
management runs the ADKAR process. They frequently don't coordinate until
after launch. The learning team must name this gap explicitly to the sponsor
before launch — not after the training fails.

---

## The Pre-Launch Gap Conversation

When change management support is absent — no Awareness campaign, no
Desire-building with managers, no Reinforcement infrastructure — the
learning leader has a professional obligation to name that gap to the
sponsor before launch.

**The principle:**

No amount or quality of training can solve for Awareness, Desire, or
Reinforcement. The sponsor must be asked to consider the entire experience
from the learner's perspective — not just the training event.

**The conversation that moves sponsors:**

Abstract language — "missing the mark," "holistic approach," "training alone
has limits" — does not move sponsors who are inclined to say "just make the
training good."

The picture that moves sponsors is specific, human, and operationally
consequential.

**The two-sentence version for a contact center unlearning scenario:**

> "What you will see in 90 days is an increase in attrition. Such a level
> of dissatisfaction that the tribal knowledge will walk out the door — and
> the ones that stay will do so on pure survivability needs."

**Why this works:**

- "Tribal knowledge walks out the door" — names the irreplaceable
  institutional knowledge loss that isn't documented anywhere; eight-year
  veterans take edge cases, workarounds, and training capacity with them
- "Pure survivability needs" — names the second-order consequence; remaining
  agents are not committed, they are trapped; that is a quality floor, not
  a quality standard
- Moves the conversation from learning outcomes → talent retention →
  operational continuity → compliance risk in two sentences
- Zero L&D jargon

## Hard-Change Design Principles for Unlearning Scenarios

1. **Diagnose resistance type before designing intervention** — cognitive,
   emotional, and behavioral resistance require different responses;
   treating them as one problem guarantees partial effectiveness at best

2. **Reframe expertise before presenting evidence** — data lands as
   persuasion only when the veteran already sees their identity preserved
   in the new approach; lead with the reframe, follow with the data

3. **Co-create with guardrails, not without them** — veteran involvement is
   the most powerful emotional resistance intervention available; it only
   works when domain boundaries are explicit and input is genuinely
   incorporated

4. **Name what training cannot do before launch** — the learning team owns
   Knowledge and Ability; Awareness, Desire, and Reinforcement belong to
   the performance environment; naming that gap is professional
   responsibility, not scope limitation

5. **Design for the performance environment, not just the training room** —
   behavioral resistance lives in the cues, rewards, and social norms of
   the job; training can build the new behavior; only the environment can
   sustain it
