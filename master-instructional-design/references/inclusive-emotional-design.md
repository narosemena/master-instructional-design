## 1. Why This Matters — The Neuroscience Foundation

### Emotion Is Not Separate from Cognition

The traditional view of learning as a purely cognitive process — information in, knowledge out — is neurologically wrong. Mary Helen Immordino-Yang's research (USC) demonstrates that emotion is not a distraction from learning; it is the biological substrate through which learning consolidates.

**The core finding:** The brain's valuation system — which tags experiences as worth encoding and remembering — is emotionally driven. Experiences that produce no emotional response (positive or negative) are neurologically tagged as unimportant and are not consolidated into long-term memory. Emotional resonance is not a "nice to have" for engagement — it is a prerequisite for encoding.

**Practical implications:**
- Learning that feels irrelevant to a learner's life and identity does not consolidate — regardless of instructional quality
- Learning that triggers threat or shame activates the amygdala, which inhibits prefrontal cortex functioning — the part of the brain responsible for complex reasoning, application, and transfer
- Learning that produces curiosity, genuine challenge, and the satisfaction of competence activates the brain's reward system — which reinforces the behavior of learning itself

**Design consequence:** The emotional arc of a learning experience is not decoration. It is instruction. Designing it thoughtlessly is the equivalent of designing a course with no practice activities — a fundamental structural failure.

### The Affective Filter (Krashen — applied to adult learning)

Originally developed for second-language acquisition, the affective filter concept translates directly to all adult learning: when anxiety, threat, embarrassment, or alienation is high, a "filter" rises that blocks input from reaching deep processing. The learner may appear to engage while actually encoding very little.

Conditions that raise the affective filter:
- High-stakes assessment before psychological safety is established
- Feeling singled out, judged, or watched
- Content that implies the learner is the problem (deficit framing)
- Lack of relevance — the learner cannot see themselves in the content
- Social threat — fear of appearing incompetent in front of peers

Conditions that lower the affective filter:
- Clear relevance to the learner's real situation
- Psychological safety — it is safe to not know and to ask
- Early wins — structured competence before challenge
- Anonymity or reduced social risk for practice
- Seeing themselves represented in the content

## 2. The Three-Lens Framework Applied at Every Stage

Every design decision should be evaluated through three lenses simultaneously — not sequentially at the end:

### Lens 1: Performance
*Does this produce the behavior change?*
Already embedded throughout the skill. The north star.

### Lens 2: Inclusion
*Who does this design serve — and who does it fail?*

Ask at each design stage:
- **Objectives:** Are the behaviors being targeted equally accessible to all learners regardless of background, ability, or prior experience?
- **Content:** Whose lived experience is centered as the default? Whose is treated as "other"?
- **Scenarios:** Who are the protagonists? What names, roles, and situations appear — and what does that communicate about who this learning is for?
- **Examples:** Are the analogies and cultural references universally accessible, or do they assume a specific demographic norm?
- **Assessment:** Does the assessment format advantage learners with certain backgrounds (academic vocabulary, test-taking experience, cultural frameworks)?
- **Technology:** Does the delivery method exclude learners with disabilities, limited bandwidth, or shared devices?

### Lens 3: Emotion
*What is the learner feeling at this moment — and is that what we want them to feel?*

Map the emotional arc alongside the content arc. For each major section of a learning experience, name:
- What emotion do we want the learner to experience here?
- What emotion might they actually experience — based on the content, format, and context?
- If those are different, what design change closes the gap?

## 3. Psychological Safety as a Design Prerequisite

Amy Edmondson's research defines psychological safety as the shared belief among team members that the environment is safe for interpersonal risk-taking — that speaking up, asking questions, admitting mistakes, or raising concerns will not result in punishment or humiliation. In learning contexts: the belief that it is safe to not know, to attempt and fail, to ask questions, and to engage honestly with difficult content.

**Psychological safety is not comfort.** It is the condition under which productive discomfort — the kind that produces growth — is possible. Learners cannot engage with genuine challenge if they first feel threatened by the environment itself.

### Design Patterns That Create Psychological Safety

**Opening design (the first 5 minutes matter most):**
- Name the learning environment explicitly: "In this program, not knowing is the starting point, not the failure condition. We're here to build, not to test."
- Use the facilitator/designer voice to model vulnerability: "Here's something I got wrong early in my career..."
- Establish norms collaboratively (for live sessions): "What do we need from each other to make this a space where we can be honest?"
- Give learners control early: choice of examples, self-pacing, ability to skip known content

**Assessment design:**
- Low-stakes retrieval practice early — build competence before requiring demonstration
- Frame wrong answers as diagnostic, not judgmental: "That's the most common misconception — here's why it feels right and what actually happens..."
- Avoid public performance before private competence is established (especially in facilitated sessions)
- Never cold-call in live sessions on sensitive topics

**Environmental signals:**
- Diverse representation in characters, facilitators, and examples signals belonging to a broader range of learners
- Language that assumes competence ("when you encounter this situation") vs. deficit language ("if you can't handle this")
- Privacy protection in assessments where personal disclosure is involved

### The Psychological Safety Gradient

Not all learning requires the same level of psychological safety. Map your program:

| Content Type | Safety Level Required | Design Implication |
|---|---|---|
| Technical skills (software, procedures) | Moderate | Clear instructions + low-stakes practice sufficient |
| Interpersonal skills (feedback, conflict) | High | Cohort trust-building before practice; safe failure scenarios |
| Values and identity (DEI, ethics, leadership) | Very High | Explicit safety contracting; facilitator skill in holding tension |
| Trauma-adjacent content (mental health, crisis, abuse) | Maximum | Trauma-informed design protocols required (see §9) |

## 4. Stereotype Threat — Recognition and Mitigation

### What Stereotype Threat Is

Claude Steele's landmark research demonstrates that when a person is aware of a negative stereotype about their identity group — and that stereotype is relevant to what they're being asked to do — their performance is measurably suppressed. This occurs even when the person does not personally endorse the stereotype, and even among high-performing individuals.

**Mechanism:** Stereotype threat consumes working memory resources. Learners spend cognitive capacity managing the threat (monitoring their performance, worrying about confirming the stereotype) — resources that would otherwise be applied to the learning task itself.

**In learning contexts, stereotype threat is activated by:**
- Being in a numerical minority in the learning group
- Assessment framing that makes identity salient ("as a woman in technology...")
- Content or scenarios that reflect the stereotype back at the learner
- Evaluative contexts where the consequences of failure feel identity-laden
- Asking members of a stereotyped group to demonstrate competence in front of members of the non-stereotyped group

### Mitigation by Design

**Reduce identity salience in high-stakes moments:**
- Avoid framing assessments with demographic identifiers ("This module is especially important for new managers" when all the examples show men)
- Don't collect demographic information immediately before a performance assessment
- Frame challenge as universal: "These scenarios are difficult for everyone — that's intentional"

**Value-affirmation exercises:**
Steele's research shows that brief value-affirmation activities (where learners write about something important to them unrelated to the domain) reduce stereotype threat effects significantly. For high-stakes learning:
- Pre-learning reflection: "Write for 5 minutes about a value that matters to you and why"
- Competence affirmation: "Think of a time you successfully learned something difficult. What did you do?"

**Representation matters at threat level:**
- Diverse characters in scenarios reduce stereotype threat for members of underrepresented groups
- Near-peer models (someone who looks like me and succeeded) are more effective than distant expert models for reducing threat
- Facilitators who share identity with stereotype-threatened groups can reduce threat for those learners — but only if they also model competence

**Belonging uncertainty:**
Closely related to stereotype threat. First-generation learners, members of minority groups, and anyone entering a new domain experiences uncertainty about whether they belong. Design interventions:
- Explicit belonging signals early: "People from all backgrounds succeed in this program"
- Stories of struggle from relatable role models — not just success stories
- Early community-building that creates genuine connection across difference

## 5. Designing the Emotional Arc

### What an Emotional Arc Is

Just as a course has a content arc (from simpler to more complex concepts), it has an emotional arc — the sequence of emotional states the learning experience is designed to produce. Most instructional designers design the content arc in detail and leave the emotional arc to chance. Expert designers design both.

### The Standard Emotional Arc for Adult Learning

**Stage 1 — Invitation (Curiosity + Relevance)**
The learner's first question is always: "Why does this matter to me?" The emotional state we want: genuine curiosity, not compliance.
- Opening hook: a problem, provocation, or story that creates a felt gap between where they are and where they want to be
- Explicit relevance bridge: "Here's how this connects to a situation you actually face"
- NOT: "Welcome to Module 3. By the end of this module you will..."

**Stage 2 — Orientation (Confidence + Safety)**
The learner needs to know they can do this. The emotional state: "I can handle this. I'm in the right place."
- Early wins: low-stakes practice that confirms basic competence before complexity
- Clear roadmap: where are we going and why does the path make sense
- Safety signal: it's OK to not know yet; that's why we're here

**Stage 3 — Challenge (Productive Discomfort + Agency)**
The learner should feel genuinely stretched — but not overwhelmed. The emotional state: challenged but not threatened; struggling but in control.
- Desirable difficulty: hard enough to require real thinking; not so hard as to produce shutdown
- Autonomy: choice in how to engage with the challenge
- Support visible but not intrusive: resources available without hand-holding

**Stage 4 — Insight (Satisfaction + Meaning)**
The moment of genuine understanding — "Oh, I get it now." The emotional state: satisfaction, pride, the click of competence.
- Design for this moment explicitly: what is the specific insight this module should produce, and how does the design create the conditions for it?
- Recognition: the design acknowledges the learner's effort and growth
- Not manufactured praise: specific, earned acknowledgment of real accomplishment

**Stage 5 — Integration (Commitment + Identity Shift)**
The learner connects the new learning to their sense of who they are and how they work. The emotional state: ownership, intention, belonging to a community of practice.
- Application commitment: a specific, self-chosen action to take back to the job
- Community: connecting with others who are applying the same learning
- Identity language: "People who do this well..." invites the learner into an identity, not just a behavior

### Mapping the Emotional Arc — Template

For each major section of a learning experience:

| Section | Content Goal | Intended Emotional State | Emotional Risk | Design Safeguard |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Opening | Establish relevance | Curiosity + safety | Boredom, compliance mode | Provocative hook; personal relevance bridge |
| Concept 1 | Build understanding | Confidence | Confusion, overwhelm | Worked example before independent practice |
| Practice 1 | Apply concept | Engagement, mild stretch | Anxiety, shame on failure | Low-stakes framing; instructional feedback |
| Concept 2 | Deepen complexity | Interest + challenge | Cognitive overload | Chunking; connect to prior concept |
| Complex scenario | Integrate concepts | Genuine effort + agency | Frustration, shutdown | Multiple attempts; hints available |
| Close | Commit to application | Ownership + belonging | Compliance ("done") | Self-chosen application goal; peer sharing |

## 6. Identity-Affirming Design

### What Identity-Affirming Design Is

Identity-affirming design goes beyond representation (diversity in images) to active affirmation — designing learning experiences that reinforce the learner's sense of themselves as capable, valued, and belonging in the domain.

**The principle:** Learning is most durable when it integrates with the learner's sense of identity. "I am someone who does this" is a more powerful retention mechanism than "I learned this fact."

### Design Practices

**Language choices:**
- "As a manager, you'll encounter..." (identity-invoking) vs. "Managers encounter..." (third-person distance)
- "People who excel at this tend to..." (aspirational identity) vs. "The correct approach is..." (compliance framing)
- Avoid deficit language: "Even if you're not technical..." signals that not being technical is the expected norm
- Use "when" not "if": "When you have this conversation with your team..." assumes competence; "If you decide to try this..." creates distance

**Scenario design:**
- Protagonists should have names from a range of cultural backgrounds — not all Western European names
- Vary the gender, age, ability status, and cultural context of characters — not just the "difficult" characters
- Avoid making the diverse character the one who makes mistakes (diversity as cautionary tale)
- Avoid making the diverse character the exotic exception ("Our newest team member, who grew up in India...")
- Scenarios should reflect the actual diversity of the industry — not just a diversity showcase moment

**Role model selection:**
- Use near-peer models (similar background, not just similar role) for identity-affirming effect
- Include stories of struggle, not just success — "I almost quit engineering in my second year" is more identity-affirming than "I knew I was going to be an engineer"
- Expert role models from underrepresented groups are powerful — but only when they're shown as fully human, not as exceptional unicorns

**Competence framing:**
- Intelligence and competence are incremental, not fixed (Carol Dweck's growth mindset research)
- Frame challenge as expected and normal: "This is genuinely difficult — that's why we practice"
- Frame mistakes as information: "That's the most common error people make — here's what it tells us"
- Avoid ability-praising feedback ("you're so good at this") in favor of process-praising feedback ("you used a really effective approach there")

## 7. DEI Content Design — Specific Guidance

### The Core Challenge

DEI content occupies a unique design territory: it is simultaneously knowledge-content (concepts, history, research) and identity-content (directly relevant to who the learner is and how they see themselves). This creates specific design risks that do not exist in other content types.

**Risk 1 — The Guilt and Shame Spiral**
Content framed primarily around what people have done wrong (microaggressions, biases, historical harm) can produce guilt and defensiveness rather than behavior change. Guilt produces compliance; genuine behavior change requires insight and motivation.

Design antidote: Lead with values ("You want an inclusive team — here's what research shows actually gets in the way of that") rather than accusation ("Here are the ways you may have caused harm").

**Risk 2 — The Single Story**
Treating diversity as monolithic — "the Black experience," "the LGBTQ+ community" — erases the diversity within diversity. This is both factually wrong and alienating to members of those groups.

Design antidote: Use specific, situated stories rather than generalized group experience. "This is one person's experience" rather than "This is what it's like to be..."

**Risk 3 — The Bystander Problem**
Framing DEI training as something that applies to "other people" who discriminate allows the majority of learners to opt out emotionally. "I'm not racist" becomes a shield against engaging with the content.

Design antidote: Design for situational engagement, not dispositional labeling. "In this situation, what would you do?" is more engaging than "Are you a biased person?"

**Risk 4 — Competence Threat**
For people in majority groups, DEI content can threaten their sense of themselves as good, fair people. When identity is threatened, learning shuts down.

Design antidote: Value-affirmation pre-activities; framing around organizational effectiveness rather than personal moral failure; acknowledging that bias is a human system property, not a character flaw.

### DEI Content Design Checklist

**Before designing:**
- [ ] Has a member of each community represented in the content reviewed the design brief?
- [ ] Do we have L3/L4 metrics — what behavior change are we actually trying to produce?
- [ ] Is training the right intervention, or is this a systemic/structural problem that training can't fix?

**In design:**
- [ ] Are scenarios specific and situated, not generic and representative?
- [ ] Are all major demographic groups represented as competent, full humans — not just as victims or as diversity props?
- [ ] Does the opening create safety before asking for personal engagement?
- [ ] Is the content grounded in learner self-interest (your team will perform better) not just moral obligation?
- [ ] Have we designed for resistance explicitly — what do we do when a learner says "This doesn't apply to me"?

**In assessment:**
- [ ] Are assessments measuring judgment and decision-making, not just knowledge of vocabulary?
- [ ] Is there a safe way to fail without public shame?
- [ ] Is self-reflection included as a legitimate form of evidence?

## 8. Learner Resistance as a Design Problem

### Reframing Resistance

When learners resist training — arrive late, disengage, argue, or simply comply without internalizing — the instinct is to label them as difficult, unmotivated, or closed-minded. This framing is almost always wrong and always unproductive.

**Resistance is information.** It tells the designer that something in the design — the topic, the framing, the environment, the facilitation, or the perceived relevance — has failed to meet the learner where they are. The appropriate response is diagnostic, not disciplinary.

### Common Sources of Resistance and Design Responses

**"I already know this."**
Source: Perception that the content is below the learner's current competence
Design response: Differentiated pathways (pre-assessment to skip known content); position the learning at an appropriate level of challenge; use the learner's existing expertise as a resource

**"This doesn't apply to me."**
Source: Failure to establish relevance to the learner's specific role, industry, or situation
Design response: Audience-specific examples and scenarios; explicit relevance bridges; self-assessment tools that help learners identify their own gap

**"This is just a compliance exercise."**
Source: The organizational context signals that the training is about protection, not development
Design response: Frame around genuine learning goals; give learners meaningful choices; ensure L4 metrics actually matter; ideally, change the organizational context — this is a culture problem, not a design problem

**"This conflicts with my values."**
Source: Content challenges a core belief or identity (most common in DEI, ethics, and values-based training)
Design response: Psychological safety first; values-affirmation pre-activities; lead with shared values rather than points of disagreement; use storytelling rather than argumentation; never try to win a debate in a training

**"I'm being set up to fail."**
Source: Assessment anxiety; history of failure in formal learning; low trust in the organization
Design response: Low-stakes early practice; transparent framing about assessment purpose; explicit "this is not a gotcha" signals; change the assessment format to lower threat

### The Resistance Design Protocol

When designing for a population with anticipated resistance:

1. **Name it:** Acknowledge in the design that this topic is one people sometimes feel defensive about or skeptical of. Naming it reduces its power.

2. **Give it air:** Create space for skepticism early — a reflection prompt, a discussion question, a pre-survey. Don't bulldoze through resistance; invite it and work with it.

3. **Lead with shared ground:** Start from values and goals the learner already holds ("You want your team to perform well. Here's what research shows gets in the way of that.") rather than from the organization's agenda.

4. **Earned trust before challenge:** Build credibility and safety with the learner before presenting the most challenging content. Don't lead with the hardest thing.

5. **Give learners agency:** Choice in how to engage, what examples to use, how to apply the content. Autonomy reduces reactance dramatically.

## 9. Trauma-Informed Learning Design

### When Trauma-Informed Design Is Required

Trauma-informed design is required when learning content involves:
- Workplace violence, harassment, or abuse (including as topics in prevention training)
- Mental health, suicide, or self-harm
- Substance use and addiction
- Grief and loss
- Discrimination and oppression (particularly for members of affected communities)
- Crisis response and emergency scenarios
- Any topic where some learners may have direct personal experience with trauma

### Core Principles (SAMHSA Framework adapted for L&D)

**Safety:** Physical and psychological safety is established before content begins. Learners know what's coming, what support is available, and how to exit without consequence.

**Trustworthiness:** The design is transparent about its purposes. Learners understand why they're being asked to engage with difficult content and how the information will be used.

**Peer Support:** Connections between learners are built before, during, and after difficult content. Isolation amplifies distress; community moderates it.

**Collaboration:** Learners have genuine input and choice. Trauma strips control; learning design that restores choice and voice is healing.

**Empowerment:** Content is framed around learner capability and strength, not vulnerability and damage. "Here's what you can do" vs. "Here's what can happen to you."

**Cultural, Historical, and Gender Issues:** Acknowledge that trauma is not experienced equally across groups. Marginalized communities experience higher rates of certain traumas and may have specific cultural frameworks for processing and healing.

### Practical Design Practices

**Content warnings that actually work:**
- Be specific, not vague: "This section includes a realistic scenario involving a workplace harassment conversation" rather than "This content may be sensitive"
- Place warnings before the content, not at the start of the course
- Provide a genuine alternative (different scenario, ability to skip) — a warning with no exit is not a warning

**Design the support system, not just the content:**
- Every trauma-adjacent module should include visible, specific support resources (EAP phone number, mental health line, HR contact)
- For facilitator-led sessions: brief the facilitator on how to handle disclosure, distress, or escalation
- Design the "after" — what happens when the session ends and learners are processing alone

**Pacing and control:**
- Shorter segments with natural break points between difficult content
- Self-paced formats preferred where possible
- "Pause and breathe" prompts are not patronizing — they're neurologically sound

## 10. Universal Design for Learning (UDL) Applied

### The Three UDL Principles

UDL provides a framework for designing learning that works for all learners from the start, rather than accommodating learners with disabilities after the fact.

**Principle 1 — Multiple Means of Representation**
Provide information in more than one format. Don't make comprehension dependent on one sensory channel.
- Audio + transcript (not just audio)
- Text + visual (not just text)
- Worked example + abstract principle (not just the principle)
- Multiple vocabulary levels (technical and accessible)

**Principle 2 — Multiple Means of Action and Expression**
Provide multiple ways for learners to engage with content and demonstrate what they know.
- Written reflection AND verbal discussion AND visual mapping — not just written quizzes
- Choice in pacing, sequencing, and depth
- Assistive technology compatibility built in from the start

**Principle 3 — Multiple Means of Engagement**
Provide multiple ways to motivate and sustain interest.
- Choice in context, examples, and application scenarios
- Varying the level of challenge (learner-controlled difficulty)
- Social AND individual learning options
- Clear purpose and relevance for every section

### Common UDL Implementation Failures

- **UDL as retrofit:** Adding a transcript after the audio is recorded, an alt-text after the image is placed. UDL as a design philosophy means the transcript is drafted in the storyboard; the alt text is part of the visual brief.
- **Representation without access:** Diverse characters in a video that has no captions. Representation matters and access matters — they are both required.
- **Multiple means as checkbox:** Three response formats that all essentially do the same thing. Multiple means of expression should offer genuinely different cognitive and physical paths.

## 11. Inclusive Facilitation — Live and Virtual

*(Extended guidance in `references/facilitation-and-ilt.md` §14. Key principles here.)*

### The Facilitation Equity Checklist

**Before:**
- [ ] Reviewed participant list for context that should inform facilitation
- [ ] All materials audited for exclusionary language, centered demographics, cultural assumptions
- [ ] Breakout groups and pairs planned intentionally (not random) to support cross-demographic connection
- [ ] Difficult scenario roles considered — who plays the "difficult customer"? Who plays the authority figure?

**During:**
- [ ] Using participation structures that distribute air time (think-pair-share, written responses, chat)
- [ ] Tracking who has spoken and actively inviting those who haven't
- [ ] Language: "folks" or "everyone" not "guys"; checking name pronunciation; using stated pronouns
- [ ] Handling harmful comments: not ignoring, not shaming — naming the impact and moving forward

**After:**
- [ ] Debrief includes space for emotional processing, not just content synthesis
- [ ] Explicit transfer commitment that doesn't assume all learners have the same workplace context

## 12. Audit Toolkit — Inclusive & Emotional Design Checklist

Use this checklist when auditing any learning artifact for inclusive and emotional design quality.

### Dimension H — DEI & Inclusive Design (extended)
- [ ] Who is centered as the default learner? Is that justified?
- [ ] Scenario characters: diverse names, roles, ages, abilities, cultural contexts?
- [ ] Language: identity-affirming, not deficit-framing, no "othering"?
- [ ] Examples and analogies accessible across cultural backgrounds?
- [ ] Assessment format accessible to learners with different learning histories?
- [ ] Technology: accessible to learners with disabilities, limited devices, variable bandwidth?
- [ ] Someone from an affected community reviewed this content?

### Dimension I — Emotional Design & Psychological Safety (extended)
- [ ] Emotional arc mapped (not just content arc)?
- [ ] Opening creates curiosity and safety, not assessment threat?
- [ ] Stage 1 (Invitation): genuine hook tied to learner's real situation?
- [ ] Stage 2 (Orientation): early win/competence before challenge?
- [ ] Stage 3 (Challenge): productive difficulty with learner agency?
- [ ] Stage 4 (Insight): the "click" moment explicitly designed?
- [ ] Stage 5 (Integration): commitment + identity shift + community?
- [ ] Stereotype threat triggers identified and mitigated?
- [ ] Resistance anticipated and designed for (not ignored)?
- [ ] Assessment feedback is instructional and dignity-preserving?
- [ ] For trauma-adjacent content: warnings, alternatives, and support resources in place?

## 13. Key Research and Texts

### Foundational Research
- **Immordino-Yang & Damasio (2007)** — Their landmark paper on the neuroscience of emotion and learning established that emotion is the biological basis of learning consolidation. Short and essential.
- **Steele (1997, 2010)** — Stereotype threat research; *Whistling Vivaldi* (2010) is the accessible book-length treatment
- **Edmondson (1999, 2018)** — Psychological safety research; *The Fearless Organization* (2018) is the practitioner application
- **Deci & Ryan (1985, 2000)** — Self-Determination Theory: autonomy, competence, and relatedness as the three basic psychological needs that motivate sustained engagement
- **Dweck (2006)** — *Mindset* — growth vs. fixed mindset; implications for how feedback, challenge, and failure are framed in learning
- **Krashen (1982)** — Affective Filter Hypothesis — extended from language acquisition to all adult learning

### Applied Texts
- **Hammond (2014)** — *Culturally Responsive Teaching and the Brain* — most practically applicable text on cultural neuroscience and learning design
- **Zaretta Hammond** — Warm demander framework: high expectations + genuine relationship = equity in learning
- **Sensoy & DiAngelo (2017)** — *Is Everyone Really Equal?* — foundational concepts for DEI content design
- **Venet (2021)** — *Equity-Centered Trauma-Informed Education* — translated into L&D contexts
- **SAMHSA (2014)** — *SAMHSA's Concept of Trauma and Guidance for a Trauma-Informed Approach* — free, foundational
