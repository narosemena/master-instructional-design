# Soft-New (Cell ④) — Design Reference

**Load when:** Classification result is Soft-New. Covers the prior scaffolding diagnostic, transfer vs. acquisition distinction, reclassification decision tree, and heterogeneous cohort design. Load alongside `references/soft-change.md` — Soft-New almost always contains a hidden Change component.

---

## The Soft-New Paradox

Organizations rarely hire for zero soft skill baseline — making Soft-New less frequent than the top three cells. However at the specific skill level, learners frequently arrive with no prior exposure to a particular soft skill application even when they have general interpersonal competence.

**The paradox:** A learner with strong general interpersonal competence is often harder to develop in a specific soft skill than a learner with no interpersonal competence at all.

**The reason:** Unlearning and relearning demands more cognitive effort than learning. Existing interpersonal patterns, habits, and instincts interfere with new specific skill acquisition even when those patterns are not formally wrong.

**Design implication:** Soft-New in practice rarely involves pure new acquisition. It almost always contains a hidden Soft-Change component that, if ignored, causes resistance to spike when the design assumed a blank slate that doesn't exist.

---

## The Prior Scaffolding Diagnostic

Before committing to a pure acquisition design on any Soft-New project, run a prior scaffolding check.

**The diagnostic question is not:** Does the learner have this specific skill?
**The diagnostic question is:** What existing interpersonal patterns, habits, and mental models will interact with this new skill during acquisition?

**How to surface this at intake — with the SME:**
Ask about past and current performance in adjacent domains.

*Example — loan judgment skill:*
*"What other types of work demand a similar performance from this population?"*

**The double move:** This question runs the diagnostic and educates the SME simultaneously. The SME's aha moment — "this isn't net new, it's an application of existing behaviors in a new context" — becomes the evidence that reclassification is needed. They arrive at the conclusion themselves.

## The Reclassification Decision Tree

When a project arrives labeled Soft-New, the prior scaffolding diagnostic produces one of four outcomes:

| Outcome | Condition | Design Path |
|---|---|---|
| **A — Pure Soft-New** | No relevant prior scaffolding exists | True acquisition — build mental models from the ground up |
| **B — Hidden Soft-Change** | Significant existing scaffolding directly conflicts with new skill | Unlearning required before acquisition begins — apply Soft-Change protocol first |
| **C — Transfer opportunity** | Relevant scaffolding exists in adjacent domain | Adapt and contextualize existing models — do not rebuild from scratch |
| **D — Mixed foundation** | Cohort is heterogeneous — some learners have relevant scaffolding, others don't | See heterogeneous cohort design below |

### Transfer vs. Acquisition — The Critical Distinction

| Approach | When to Use | Failure Mode if Misapplied |
|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition** | True Soft-New learners with no relevant prior scaffolding | Transfer-ready learners disengage; foundational work they don't need crowds out application work they do |
| **Transfer** | Learners with adjacent experience | Assumes scaffolding that doesn't exist; application work collapses because the mental model underneath was never built |

## Designing for the Heterogeneous Cohort (Outcome D)

When the cohort contains both experienced learners with relevant scaffolding and novices without it:

**First recommendation:** Separate the populations into distinct interventions if resources allow. Each population has a different design need and serves the other poorly when combined without structure.

**When separation is not possible — the expert augmenter model:**

Position experienced learners as augmenters of content for novice learners. Experts become a design asset rather than a design problem.

**Structural constraint — cross-level pairing:**
- Experts must be partnered with novices, never with other experts
- Expert-expert pairing produces echo chambers where existing scaffolding is reinforced rather than examined
- Novice-novice pairing produces acquisition without transfer modeling
- Cross-level pairing: novice builds mental model, expert adapts theirs through the act of explaining it

**The explaining mechanism:**
When an expert explains their existing judgment process to a novice, they externalize tacit knowledge. That externalization frequently surfaces the adaptation delta — the gap between what they currently do and what the new context requires — that was invisible while the knowledge remained implicit.

## Activity Design — Cross-Level Pairs

**Scenario:** Loan judgment skill — cross-level pair of one experienced analyst with adjacent judgment experience and one brand new analyst with no prior scaffolding.

**Activity: Persona Card Exercise**

**Materials:** Deck of persona cards representing all possible credit applicants — each card contains profile information about an applicant.

### Sequence

**Step 1 — Pair work (unstructured)**
Expert and novice examine persona cards together. Task: identify the elements that should inform the judgment decision for each persona. No facilitator intervention. Hash it out.

*Design rationale:* Unstructured pair time surfaces the expert's scaffolding naturally through conversation. The novice's questions probe tacit knowledge in ways facilitator questions cannot. The adaptation delta often surfaces organically here.

**Step 2 — Group debrief**
Reconvene the full cohort. Pull on aha moments from novices first — their surprise is real-time diagnostic data showing where mental models were incomplete or absent. Then probe veterans for additional expertise.

**Step 3 — Closed veteran probe**
*"Veteran, in your experience, what is the most important factor from the elements you read on the cards that helps your decision process?"*

*Design rationale:* Closed question forces prioritization — the veteran must answer with a specific judgment criterion, not a story. That criterion becomes the next instructional moment.

**Step 4 — Open to room**
*"Excellent — anyone else have a similar experience, or even a different one?"*

*Design rationale:* Opens the criterion to examination without editorializing. "Similar or different" signals both responses are equally welcome — prevents premature convergence on one expert's framework as the definitive answer.

**Step 5 — Surface the veteran's pain**
*"Veteran, what challenges do you regularly face when trying to get a true picture of the applicant? What would you change or do differently? What would you like to have?"*

*Design rationale:* The veteran names the limitations of their current judgment process in their own language. When the new model is introduced immediately after, it lands as an answer to their stated need — not as an imposed framework. The novice observes an expert modeling intellectual humility without being asked to.

**Step 6 — Introduce the new model as amplifier**
Frame the new model's value in terms of the veteran's stated pain — it makes the factors they already judge by more visible, faster, with less cognitive effort. The veteran's judgment criteria are preserved. The new model amplifies them.

*Design rationale:* The adaptation delta is made visible without being named as a deficit. The veteran arrives at "this makes my judgment easier" themselves.

## Debrief Guardrail — Preventing Expert Dominance

**Risk:** Veterans dominate with undirected storytelling; novices become audience rather than participants.

**Solution:** Closed, targeted questions aimed at specific veterans extract bounded answers rather than opening narrative doors. Each answer becomes the next instructional moment — the facilitator builds on it rather than opening general discussion.

**The facilitation pattern:**
1. Closed question to veteran → specific criterion or insight extracted
2. Open to room → similar or different experiences surface
3. Probe for pain → veteran names the limitation of current approach
4. Introduce amplifier → new model positioned as solution to stated need
5. Return to novice → what surprised you? what questions does this raise?

The novice's questions and surprises anchor the debrief to acquisition needs. The veteran's insights advance the transfer work. Both populations are active throughout.

## Soft-New Design Principles

1. **Always run the prior scaffolding diagnostic before committing to acquisition design** — Soft-New projects almost always contain hidden Change components; misclassification produces resistance the design cannot explain

2. **Transfer and acquisition are different design problems** — building mental models from scratch for learners who already have them produces disengagement; assuming scaffolding that doesn't exist produces collapse

3. **Heterogeneous cohorts require structural solutions, not just facilitation skill** — cross-level pairing is a design decision, not an improvisation; the structure must be built into the activity before the room convenes

4. **The expert's pain is the new model's entry point** — asking experienced learners to name the limitations of their current approach before introducing the new framework produces owned adoption rather than compliance

5. **Novice surprise is diagnostic data** — what surprises novices in pair work and debrief reveals where mental model construction is incomplete; treat it as real-time formative assessment
