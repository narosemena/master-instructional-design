## 1. ILT vs. VILT vs. eLearning — When to Use Each

The right modality is a design decision, not a convenience decision. Drive it from learner need and performance outcome.

| Modality | Best For | Watch Out For |
|---|---|---|
| **ILT (In-person)** | Complex interpersonal skills; culture-building; high-stakes practice with coaching; onboarding cohorts; leadership development | Cost, scalability, consistency across facilitators |
| **VILT (Virtual ILT)** | Geographically distributed teams; interactive live discussion; cohort connection without travel cost | Tech fatigue; passive Zoom attendance; engagement drop after 45 min |
| **eLearning (Async)** | Knowledge transfer; procedural skills; compliance; consistent delivery at scale | No live coaching; isolation; no social learning |
| **Blended** | Complex multi-week programs; combining knowledge-build (async) + skill practice (live) | Design complexity; requires more project management |
| **Performance Support** | On-the-job reference; rare tasks; high-stakes procedures; point-of-need guidance | Not a substitute for skill development |

**Decision filter for live vs. async:**
- Does the learner need real-time feedback and coaching? → Live
- Does the content require practice with another human? → Live
- Is social learning and peer exchange part of the value? → Live
- Is it primarily knowledge transfer the learner controls? → Async first
- Is it highly procedural and better done at the moment of need? → Performance support

## 2. Workshop & ILT Design Architecture

### The Four-Phase Workshop Structure

**Phase 1 — Open (10–15% of time)**
Purpose: Create psychological safety, establish relevance, activate prior knowledge
- Opening hook: a problem, provocation, story, or data point — never "let's review the agenda"
- Relevance bridge: connect the session explicitly to what learners care about
- Prior knowledge activation: what do they already know/believe about this? (poll, pair share, sticky notes)
- Set norms collaboratively, not directively

**Phase 2 — Explore (30–40% of time)**
Purpose: Build understanding through active engagement with content — not lecture
- Jigsaw learning: groups become experts, then teach each other
- Case study analysis: pairs/trios diagnose a realistic situation before seeing the model
- Structured discovery: present a problem; let learners grapple before revealing framework
- Minimise direct instruction time to 10–15 minutes maximum before an activity

**Phase 3 — Practice (30–40% of time)**
Purpose: Apply learning to realistic situations with coaching feedback
- Role plays, simulations, scenario analysis — grounded in real work contexts
- Deliberate practice: not just doing, but doing with feedback and reflection
- Structured debrief after every practice: what happened? why? what would you do differently?
- Observer roles: non-participants observe and give structured feedback

**Phase 4 — Close (10–15% of time)**
Purpose: Consolidate learning, commit to application, close the experience
- Synthesis activity: individual reflection on key insight + one commitment to change
- Application planning: specific, observable action before next session/back on the job
- Peer accountability: share commitment with a partner who will follow up
- Close the loop on the opening hook — what's the answer now?

### ILT Sequencing Principles
- **Simple before complex**: don't introduce advanced application before foundational concepts are established
- **Cognitive before affective**: build understanding before challenging values or identity
- **Individual before group**: give thinking time before asking for public contribution
- **Low stakes before high stakes**: confidence-building activities before high-visibility role plays
- **Concrete before abstract**: ground theory in experience and examples before naming the framework

### Timing Guidelines
- Maximum unbroken direct instruction: 10–12 minutes
- Activity debrief time = at least 50% of activity time (if activity takes 15 min, debrief takes 8–10 min)
- Breaks: every 75–90 minutes for full-day sessions; every 50–60 minutes for half-day
- Buffer: build 15% buffer into all session timings — activities always run longer than planned

## 3. The Facilitation Guide — Format & Standards

A facilitation guide is not a script. It is a navigation tool that enables a skilled facilitator to deliver consistently and adapt in real time.

### Standard Facilitation Guide Structure

**Header block** (per section/module):
- Section title and time allocation (e.g., "Opening — 20 minutes")
- Learning objective(s) for the section
- Materials needed: slides (slide numbers), handouts, supplies
- Setup requirements: room arrangement, tech check, pre-printed materials

**Per-activity block format:**
```
ACTIVITY: [Name]
Time: [X minutes]
Purpose: [Why this activity; what it accomplishes]
Materials: [Slide X, Handout Y, sticky notes]

FACILITATOR INSTRUCTIONS:
Step 1: [Exact setup and transition language]
Step 2: [Instructions to give participants — exact wording in quotes]
Step 3: [Monitor phase — what to watch for, how to coach]
Step 4: [Debrief — specific questions, not "how did that go?"]

DEBRIEF QUESTIONS:
- [Question 1 — surface the learning]
- [Question 2 — connect to real work]
- [Question 3 — identify application]

FACILITATOR NOTES:
- Common misconceptions to address
- If group is resistant: [specific response]
- If group finishes early: [extension option]
- If group runs long: [cut option]

TRANSITION: [Exact bridge language to next section]
```

### Facilitation Guide Tone & Language Conventions
- Write facilitator instructions in second person imperative: "Ask participants to..." not "The facilitator should ask..."
- Put exact words to say in **bold** or quotation marks
- Flag timing pressure points with ⏱ symbols
- Mark "do not skip" moments with 🔴
- Include slide numbers in brackets: [Slide 14]

## 4. Live Learning Activity Library

### Discussion & Reflection Activities

**Think-Pair-Share**
Pose a question → 2 min individual reflection → 3 min partner discussion → share with room
Best for: surfacing prior knowledge, generating diverse perspectives before presenting a model

**Fishbowl**
Inner circle (4–6 people) discusses while outer circle observes; then debrief with full group
Best for: modeling a skill, exploring controversy, surfacing different perspectives in a visible way

**Socratic Seminar**
Facilitator-led inquiry through a text, case, or data set; no right answers; building thinking together
Best for: leadership development, ethics, complex ambiguous problems

**Chalk Talk / Silent Discussion**
Post questions on chart paper; participants respond in writing and respond to each other's responses
Best for: introverted groups; generating ideas without social pressure; complex emotional topics

**Plus/Delta**
Quick close: what went well (+) and what to change (Δ) for next time
Best for: rapid session or program evaluation

### Practice & Application Activities

**Role Play — Structured**
Assign roles + scenario cards; observer sheet with specific behaviors to look for; structured debrief
Best for: sales conversations, difficult feedback, customer service, coaching skills

**Fishbowl Role Play**
One pair practices in the center; rest observe with structured observer sheet; rotate
Best for: coaching skills, performance conversations, any interpersonal skill — reduces pressure of being watched by everyone simultaneously

**Case Study Analysis**
Small groups analyze a realistic case against a framework; present findings; facilitate class synthesis
Best for: problem-solving, applying new frameworks, diagnostic thinking, management scenarios

**World Café**
3–4 tables each exploring a different question; participants rotate every 15–20 minutes; ideas build
Best for: large groups generating diverse ideas; strategy sessions; knowledge-sharing across teams

**Action Planning**
Individual planning template: What will I do? By when? How will I know it worked? Who will support me?
Best for: closing a program; increasing transfer probability

**Skill Stations / Learning Lab**
Participants rotate through stations, each with a different practice scenario or challenge
Best for: procedural skills, technical training, simulations at scale

### Knowledge-Build Activities

**Jigsaw**
Each group becomes expert on one piece of content, then teaches other groups
Best for: distributing large amounts of content; increasing engagement over lecture

**Gallery Walk**
Post content/questions on walls; participants walk and annotate; debrief what they noticed
Best for: large amounts of visual content; generating questions; low-energy moments in the day

**Quiz Show / Kahoot-style**
Competitive or collaborative knowledge check with scoring
Best for: energizing; reinforcing retention; making compliance content bearable

## 5. VILT Platform Strategy & Facilitation Techniques

### Platform Comparison for VILT

| Feature Need | Zoom | Microsoft Teams | Webex | Adobe Connect |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Breakout rooms | ✅ Strong | ✅ Good | ✅ Good | ✅ Advanced |
| Polling | ✅ Built-in | ✅ Forms integration | ✅ Built-in | ✅ Built-in |
| Whiteboard/Annotate | ✅ Zoom Whiteboard | ✅ Whiteboard | ✅ Webex Board | ✅ Advanced |
| Persistent chat | ❌ Clears on exit | ✅ Persistent | ⚠️ Limited | ⚠️ Limited |
| Recording | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ | ✅ |
| Engagement tools | 🟡 Moderate | 🟡 Moderate | 🟡 Moderate | ✅ Richest |
| External integrations | ✅ Miro, Mural, Slido | ✅ Wide | ⚠️ Limited | ⚠️ Limited |

**Third-party engagement tools that integrate with any platform:**
- **Miro / Mural**: Virtual whiteboard; collaborative; excellent for workshop activities
- **Slido**: Polling, Q&A, word clouds, quizzes — highest-quality audience engagement tool
- **Mentimeter**: Real-time polling and word clouds
- **Padlet**: Digital sticky notes for brainstorming and gallery walks
- **Jamboard** (Google): Simple collaborative whiteboard for Google Workspace users

### VILT Design Principles
- **60-minute maximum** for a single unbroken VILT session before a break or hard stop — cognitive fatigue is real
- **Interaction every 4–6 minutes** — without this, attendance becomes passive background noise
- **Camera-on culture**: establish early, model it yourself, frame it as a learning community norm — not surveillance
- **Producer role**: for groups over 20, a producer manages breakouts/tech while facilitator focuses on learning
- **Bandwidth design**: never assume everyone has stable high bandwidth — audio > video; design for the worst connection

### VILT Interaction Pattern Toolkit

**Chat waterfall**: Pose a question → "Everyone type your answer but don't hit Enter yet → on my signal, everyone hit Enter at once"
Effect: Simultaneously surfaces all responses; prevents anchoring to first answer

**Non-verbal signals**: Raise hand 👋, yes/no thumbs, green check/red X for pace-checking
Use sparingly as punctuation, not constantly — over-signaling is patronizing

**Annotate/stamp**: Ask participants to stamp or annotate a shared image, diagram, or question
Best for: decision mapping, temperature checks, spatial thinking activities

**Breakout debrief protocol**: After breakouts, don't just ask "what did you discuss?" — assign a specific reporter and a specific question: "In 60 seconds: what was your most important insight and one remaining question?"

**Live document collaboration**: Share a Google Doc or Miro board; multiple groups contribute simultaneously in real time — more dynamic than waiting for group report-outs

## 6. Managing Group Dynamics & Difficult Situations

### Common Facilitation Challenges

**The Dominant Talker**
- "Thank you — let's hear from someone who hasn't spoken yet"
- Use structured activities (think-pair-share, written responses) that limit air time by design
- In breakouts, rotate the reporter role so no one person always presents

**The Silent Group**
- Don't rush to fill silence — wait 8–10 seconds after a question before rephrasing
- Lower the stakes: individual written reflection before public sharing; partner share before group share
- Check your question: "Does anyone have thoughts?" is a weak question; "Take 60 seconds — write one word that captures your reaction to this scenario" is not

**The Resistant Participant**
- Name it non-defensively: "It sounds like you have some concerns about this — can you say more?"
- Find what's valid in the resistance and acknowledge it before moving on
- Avoid public debates — take contentious disagreements offline after the session

**The Expert Who Challenges the Facilitator**
- "That's a great point — what does the group think?"
- Use their expertise as a resource: "You have experience with this — would you be willing to share an example?"
- Never pretend to know something you don't — "I'd want to look that up before answering definitively"

**The Derailing Side Conversation**
- "I want to make sure we capture that — let me write it on the parking lot and we'll come back to it"
- In VILT: message the person privately in chat rather than calling them out publicly

**Low Energy / Post-Lunch Slump**
- Physical movement: stand and stretch, change pairs, move to different stations
- Competition: quick quiz, team challenge, or racing activity
- Never try to lecture through low energy — shift to an activity

## 7. Train-the-Trainer Program Design

### The TTT Competency Model — What Facilitators Need

A well-designed TTT program addresses three dimensions:

**Know** — Content mastery
- Deep understanding of the subject matter (not just the course content)
- Common questions, misconceptions, and edge cases
- Background context the participant guide doesn't contain

**Do** — Facilitation skill
- How to open, manage transitions, run each activity, and close
- How to debrief — not just "how did it go?" but specific facilitation questioning
- How to read the room and adapt
- How to manage difficult participants and situations

**Be** — Facilitator mindset
- Learner-centered, not content-delivery-centered
- Comfort with not knowing: "I'll find out and come back to you"
- Psychological safety: modeling vulnerability and authentic engagement

### TTT Program Architecture

**Phase 1: Content Deep Dive (Pre-work or Day 1)**
- SME-led content immersion beyond what's in the materials
- Q&A: every question a participant could ask, answered
- Common misconceptions and how to address them

**Phase 2: Facilitation Guide Walkthrough**
- Annotated walkthrough of the full facilitation guide
- Timing guidance, transition language, activity logistics
- "Why we designed it this way" context — so facilitators understand intent, not just mechanics

**Phase 3: Skill Practice with Coaching**
- Each facilitator practices a section while others observe with structured observer sheets
- Debrief using Pendleton's model: self-assessment → observer strengths → suggestions
- Multiple rounds until fluency is established
- Video recording for self-review (powerful when participants watch themselves)

**Phase 4: Certification**
- Live facilitation of a full section observed by a master facilitator or L&D lead
- Checklist-based: not subjective pass/fail, but specific behavioral criteria
- Calibration: all certifiers use the same standards; inter-rater reliability matters

### Facilitator Certification Checklist (Sample Criteria)
- [ ] Opens session with an engaging hook (not agenda review)
- [ ] States learning objectives in learner-benefit language
- [ ] Gives activity instructions clearly before asking for questions
- [ ] Manages time within ±5 minutes of guide
- [ ] Debriefs with specific questions, not "thoughts?"
- [ ] Responds to resistance without defensiveness
- [ ] Uses names; makes eye contact across the room
- [ ] Transitions cleanly between sections
- [ ] Closes with commitment/application activity

## 8. Blended Learning Architecture

### The 70-20-10 Framework Applied to Blended Design
- **70% on-the-job**: Design for application — manager coaching, stretch assignments, performance support at the moment of need
- **20% social/peer**: Communities of practice, cohort check-ins, peer coaching, mentoring
- **10% formal**: eLearning modules, ILT workshops, VILT sessions

Blended design error: spending 90% of design effort on the 10% formal learning while ignoring the 70%.

### Blended Program Design Sequence

**Pre-work** (1–2 weeks before live session)
Purpose: Activate prior knowledge, establish baseline, create investment
- Short eLearning module (15–20 min): foundational concepts only
- Reflection prompt: "Bring one real example from your work that relates to [topic]"
- Optional: pre-assessment or survey to customize the live session

**Live Session** (ILT or VILT)
Purpose: Practice, application, coaching, peer learning — NOT re-teaching pre-work
- Assume pre-work was completed; don't re-cover it
- Use session for what only live can do: practice, feedback, discussion, edge cases

**Post-work / Application** (2–4 weeks after)
Purpose: Transfer to the job, reinforce learning, prevent forgetting
- Job aid deployed to moment-of-need
- Spaced retrieval: short nudges or quizzes at Day 3, Day 7, Day 21
- Manager coaching guide: 3 questions managers ask their direct report after training
- Accountability: peer check-in or cohort forum post — "share one thing you tried"

**Sustain** (ongoing)
- Community of practice touchpoints
- Advanced module for those ready for more
- Recognition of application

## 9. Needs Analysis — Methods & Tools

### The Needs Analysis Process

**Step 1 — Initial Intake (Stakeholder Conversation)**
When a stakeholder says "I need a training on X," your first move is not to start designing. It is to understand the performance problem behind the request.

Key questions:
- "What will people be doing differently after the training that they're not doing now?"
- "How do you know there's a problem? What are you seeing or measuring?"
- "What happens when someone does this wrong? What's the cost?"
- "Tell me about someone who does this really well. What do they do differently?"
- "Have we ever trained on this before? What happened?"
- "If you waved a magic wand and fixed this perfectly — what would be different in 6 months?"

**Step 2 — Root Cause Analysis**
Before recommending a solution, diagnose the cause. Gilbert's Behavior Engineering Model:

| Cause | Intervention (Not Training) |
|---|---|
| No clear expectations/standards | Clarify expectations, standards, job descriptions |
| No feedback on performance | Add feedback mechanisms, dashboards, manager check-ins |
| No consequences for poor performance | Performance management, recognition, accountability |
| Environmental barriers (tools, time, resources) | Remove barriers, provide resources |
| **Knowledge/skill gap** | **Training is appropriate** |
| Motivation/attitude (despite skill) | Coaching, culture, selection, management |

**Step 3 — Data Collection Methods**

| Method | Best For | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| **Stakeholder interviews** | Strategic context, business goals, political landscape | Stakeholder bias; may not know what performers actually do |
| **Performer interviews** | Task detail, common challenges, real examples | Self-report bias; may describe ideal, not actual behavior |
| **Focus groups** | Shared experiences, common themes across a population | Dominant voices; social desirability |
| **Observation** | Actual behavior in context; what people really do vs. say they do | Time-intensive; observer effect |
| **Surveys/questionnaires** | Scaling data collection; quantifying frequency and importance of tasks | Shallow; limited by question quality |
| **Work product analysis** | Objective performance evidence; identifying error patterns | Requires access; interpretation requires context |
| **Performance data review** | Error rates, quality scores, customer complaints, time-to-competency | Lagging indicators; doesn't explain cause |

## 10. Task Analysis Techniques — Full Reference

### Hierarchical Task Analysis (HTA)
**Use for**: Step-by-step procedural tasks with a clear correct sequence
**Process**:
1. Define the goal (the top-level task: "Complete a customer refund")
2. Identify sub-goals (major phases: verify purchase, process credit, document transaction)
3. Decompose each sub-goal into operations (individual observable actions)
4. Define the plan (the sequence and conditions under which steps occur)
5. Stop decomposing when steps are at the level a new performer needs instruction

**Output**: Hierarchical diagram or numbered outline showing the full task structure
**ID application**: Each terminal task becomes a learning objective candidate; the hierarchy reveals prerequisite relationships

### Cognitive Task Analysis (CTA)
**Use for**: Expert judgment, decision-making, diagnosis, tasks where the critical steps happen invisibly in the expert's head
**Process**:
1. **Critical Decision Method (CDM)**: Interview experts about challenging incidents — not hypotheticals. "Tell me about a time when X was especially difficult. Walk me through exactly what you were thinking."
2. Probe for: cues noticed, options considered, decision criteria, knowledge used, common errors novices make
3. Create a decision requirements table: situations → cues → decisions → actions → knowledge needed

**Output**: Decision flowcharts, worked examples, scenario structures that model expert thinking
**ID application**: The content of CTA becomes the scenarios, case studies, and decision simulations in the course

### Job Task Analysis (JTA)
**Use for**: Building a curriculum for a full role; prioritizing what to include when content is vast
**Process**:
1. Identify all tasks performed in the role (through interviews, observation, existing documentation)
2. Survey a sample of performers and supervisors: rate each task on **frequency** (how often performed) and **criticality** (impact of error)
3. Plot tasks on a 2×2 (frequency × criticality) — high/high = must train; high/low = performance support; low/high = ensure competence; low/low = exclude or reference only
4. Verify with stakeholders before finalizing curriculum scope

**Output**: Prioritized task inventory; justified curriculum scope
**ID application**: Prevents content bloat; gives you evidence-based rationale for what's in and out of scope

### Content Analysis
**Use for**: Knowledge-heavy subjects with lots of declarative content (regulations, policies, technical concepts)
**Process**:
1. Classify all content into: **facts** (specific, memorized), **concepts** (categories with examples/non-examples), **principles** (cause-effect relationships, rules), **procedures** (steps), **attitudes** (values, dispositions)
2. Map each content type to the appropriate instructional strategy:
   - Facts → repetition, memory aids
   - Concepts → examples + non-examples; classification practice
   - Principles → if-then practice; case application
   - Procedures → demonstration + practice; job aid
   - Attitudes → modeling, stories, reflection, safe practice

**Output**: Content type matrix; instructional strategy map
**ID application**: Prevents one-size-fits-all instruction; ensures content type drives method selection

## 11. Needs Analysis Interview Templates

### Stakeholder Interview Guide (45–60 minutes)

**Opening** (5 min):
"Thanks for your time. My goal today is to understand the performance challenge deeply enough to recommend the right solution — which may or may not be a training. The more honest and specific you can be, the better I can help."

**Section 1 — The Problem** (15 min):
1. "What specifically prompted this request — what are you seeing or hearing that tells you there's a gap?"
2. "How long has this been a problem? Is it getting worse, better, or staying the same?"
3. "What's the cost or risk of not solving it? What happens if we do nothing?"
4. "How widespread is this? Is it across the whole population or concentrated in certain groups/locations?"

**Section 2 — The Performers** (10 min):
5. "Tell me about someone who does this well. What do they do differently from someone who struggles?"
6. "What do new people typically get wrong? What's the most common error?"
7. "What do most people believe or assume about this topic that gets in the way?"

**Section 3 — The Context** (10 min):
8. "Walk me through a typical situation where this performance gap shows up — what's the scenario?"
9. "What tools, systems, or resources are available on the job when this task needs to be done?"
10. "What gets in the way of performing well even when someone knows what to do?"

**Section 4 — History & Constraints** (10 min):
11. "Have we addressed this before? What happened? What worked or didn't?"
12. "What are the constraints — timeline, budget, audience size, technology?"
13. "How will we know this has been solved? What will you see or measure 6 months from now?"

**Closing** (5 min):
"Based on what you've told me, my preliminary thinking is [X]. I want to do a few performer interviews and then come back to you with a recommendation. Does that work?"

### Performer Interview Guide (30 minutes)

**Purpose**: Understand the actual work, not the ideal. What do top performers actually do?

1. "Walk me through a recent time you had to [perform target task]. What did you do first?"
2. "What information or cues do you rely on when making decisions about [X]?"
3. "What's the hardest part of this? What trips people up?"
4. "Have you ever seen someone get this wrong? What happened? What should they have done instead?"
5. "What do you wish you'd known when you were new to this role?"
6. "What tools or references do you use when you're unsure? Are they easy to find?"
7. "Is there anything about the environment — systems, processes, management — that makes this harder than it should be?"

## 12. Performance Support & Job Aid Design

### When Performance Support Is the Right Solution
Performance support belongs at the moment of need — not in a course. Design it for:
- Tasks done infrequently (can't rely on memory)
- High-stakes tasks where errors are costly
- Complex procedures with many steps or decision branches
- New system/process rollouts where recall is unreliable
- Situations where a trained skill is available but environmental cues are needed

### Job Aid Types & When to Use Each

| Type | Best For | Format |
|---|---|---|
| **Step-by-step checklist** | Sequential procedures; compliance tasks; safety protocols | Numbered list; checkbox format |
| **Decision tree / flowchart** | Branching decisions; troubleshooting; diagnosis | Visual flowchart; if/then logic |
| **Reference card / cheat sheet** | Frequently needed facts, formulas, codes, shortcuts | Card, laminated, QR-linked |
| **Process map** | Understanding how a process works; cross-functional visibility | Visual workflow diagram |
| **Comparison table** | Choosing between options; product/service differentiation | Table with key criteria |
| **Annotated screenshot** | Software tasks; system navigation | Screenshot with callouts |
| **Video micro-demo** | Short (90–120 second) show-me demonstrations | Short video, searchable library |

### Job Aid Design Principles
- **Designed for the moment of use, not the classroom**: The person using it is at their desk, possibly stressed, needing to act. Design for speed and clarity.
- **Test with a real user before publishing**: If a performer can't use it without asking a question, it's not done.
- **One job aid = one task**: Don't cram multiple unrelated tasks onto one aid. Better to have three focused aids than one cluttered one.
- **Plain language required**: Active voice, short sentences, no jargon the performer doesn't use, no organizational acronyms without definition.
- **Visible and accessible**: A job aid no one can find is useless. Design the distribution strategy as part of the design.

### Job Aid Quick-Test Checklist
- [ ] Can a performer find the right information in under 30 seconds?
- [ ] Are all steps/decisions at the level of observable action?
- [ ] Is it free of training-context language ("as we discussed...")?
- [ ] Has a real performer reviewed it for accuracy and usability?
- [ ] Is there a plan for where/how it will be accessed on the job?

## 13. Microlearning Design

### What Microlearning Is (and Isn't)
**Is**: Short, focused learning assets (2–7 minutes) targeting one specific performance need, delivered close to the moment of need, often as part of a spaced practice strategy or performance support ecosystem.

**Isn't**: A full course chopped into small pieces. That's just a short course.

### When Microlearning Works
- Reinforcing a concept introduced in formal training (spaced repetition)
- Performance support at the moment of need (specific, task-focused)
- Short knowledge updates (policy change, product update, compliance reminder)
- Embedded in a workflow (push notification, LMS nudge, email drip)

### When Microlearning Doesn't Work
- Complex skill development requiring practice and feedback
- Deep conceptual understanding requiring extended engagement
- When the learner has zero prior context on the topic
- As a substitute for robust learning design when budget is the real driver

### Microlearning Design Standards
- **One objective per asset**: One thing the learner can do/know/apply after this 3-minute piece
- **Context first**: Open with the "why this matters right now" — not "welcome to this module on..."
- **Active over passive**: Even in 3 minutes, include a decision, a scenario, or a question — not just a video to watch
- **Retrieval practice format**: Question → attempt → feedback → explanation is more effective than explanation → question
- **Search-optimized titles**: Microlearning is only useful if learners can find it; "How to Handle a Refund Request" beats "Module 4B"

### Spaced Retrieval Campaign Design
Week 1 (3 days post-training): First reinforcement — brief scenario question on key concept
Week 2 (10 days post-training): Second reinforcement — slightly harder application question
Week 3 (21 days post-training): Third reinforcement — complex scenario requiring full skill integration
Week 6 (45 days post-training): Final check — confidence + competence self-assessment; flag gaps for manager

Tools: Axonify, Qstream, Nudge, or a simple LMS quiz drip configured with release dates.

## 14. DEI & Inclusive Facilitation Practice

### Facilitator Responsibilities in Inclusive Learning Environments

**Before the session:**
- Review participant list for any context that should inform facilitation (language backgrounds, accessibility needs, known group dynamics)
- Audit all materials for stereotyping, exclusive language, centered demographics
- Design activities for multiple ways of participating: writing, speaking, visual, movement

**During the session:**

**Psychological safety behaviors:**
- Model vulnerability: share genuine uncertainty, not just polished answers
- Name the discomfort explicitly when discussing sensitive topics: "This is a topic where people have strong reactions — that's expected and OK here"
- Respond to wrong answers as useful data, not failure: "That's a common assumption — let's look at what the evidence says"

**Equitable participation:**
- Use structured activities (think-pair-share, written response, breakout groups) to interrupt the pattern of loud voices dominating
- Track who has spoken — call on people who haven't, not just volunteers
- "I'd love to hear from someone who hasn't weighed in yet"

**Responding to bias in the room:**
- When a participant makes a biased or harmful statement, don't ignore it and don't shame them
- "I want to pause on that — that perspective is one we sometimes hear. Let's look at what the data shows" (redirect to evidence)
- For more serious moments: name what happened clearly, reaffirm the norm, and move forward — "That landed in a way that can feel excluding. Let's make sure our conversation honors everyone in the room"

**Language practices:**
- Use people-first language unless the community uses identity-first (ask)
- Avoid "guys" as a gender-neutral plural — use "folks," "everyone," "team"
- Don't assume shared cultural references (sports, holidays, idioms) — use workplace examples
- Check in on pronunciation of names: "I want to make sure I say your name right"

### Inclusive Design Audit — Five Questions
1. Would a learner from any demographic represented in this audience see themselves reflected in this content?
2. Are the "default" characters (unnamed protagonists, example employees) varied — or are they implicitly white, male, able-bodied, and Western?
3. Does the content inadvertently center certain lived experiences as "normal" and others as exceptions?
4. Are the scenarios and examples relevant to the full diversity of the workforce — not just the majority group?
5. Has someone with a different background from the design team reviewed this content before launch?
